# Plants

Plants are the dominant primary producers in terrestrial ecosystems, converting solar energy into biomass that funds nearly all heterotrophic life. From a systems perspective, they are the interface between abiotic energy input and the biotic economy — the tap through which energy enters food webs. They are also the paradigmatic sessile organisms, and their immobility shapes every strategy they employ: defense, reproduction, competition, and environmental modification all operate under the constraint that a plant cannot leave.

## Systemic role

Plants occupy the base of the trophic pyramid. Their systemic role is threefold:

**Energy gateway.** Terrestrial net primary productivity (NPP) is approximately 120 Pg C/year globally (Field et al. 1998), virtually all of it plant-driven. Every joule available to herbivores, predators, and decomposers passes through plant biomass first. Remove plants and the entire energy flow collapses — not gradually, but structurally. There is no alternative pathway for solar energy to enter the food web at comparable scale.

**Biomass reservoir.** Plants hold roughly 450 Gt C of the biosphere's ~550 Gt C total living biomass (Bar-On, Phillips & Milo 2018). They are the largest standing stock of energy in any terrestrial ecosystem. This stock has residence times measured in years to centuries (wood), making plants a slow variable in system dynamics — they buffer and smooth energy availability for consumers operating on faster timescales.

**Habitat structure.** Plants physically construct the environment other organisms inhabit. Canopy architecture determines light regimes, root systems structure soil, leaf litter creates the detrital layer. This is ecosystem engineering in the sense of Jones, Lawton & Shachak (1994) — plants modify abiotic conditions, creating niches that would not otherwise exist.

In stocks-and-flows terms:

- **Stocks**: standing biomass (aboveground and belowground), seed banks, soil organic matter derived from plant inputs.
- **Inflows**: photosynthetic carbon fixation (gross primary productivity), nutrient uptake from soil.
- **Outflows**: respiration (roughly 50% of GPP), herbivory, litterfall, mortality, root exudates.
- **Turnover**: leaf turnover (annual in deciduous systems), fine root turnover (months to years), wood turnover (decades to centuries).

## Energy and nutrient flows

### Carbon and energy

Solar radiation strikes the canopy. Of the photosynthetically active radiation (PAR, 400-700nm), plants capture roughly 1-2% in gross primary productivity (GPP) under natural conditions (Monteith 1972). Approximately half of GPP is consumed by plant respiration (autotrophic respiration), leaving net primary productivity (NPP) as the energy available to the rest of the ecosystem.

NPP flows outward through several channels:

1. **Herbivory**: Typically 5-15% of terrestrial NPP is consumed by herbivores in most ecosystems (Cyr & Pace 1993), though this can spike to 50%+ during outbreaks.
2. **Litterfall and mortality**: The majority of NPP enters the detrital pathway — dead leaves, wood, roots. This funds the decomposer economy.
3. **Root exudates**: Plants leak 5-20% of fixed carbon through roots into the rhizosphere (Bais et al. 2006), feeding mycorrhizal networks and soil microbiota. This is not waste — it is investment in nutrient acquisition infrastructure.
4. **Volatile organic compounds**: A smaller but ecologically significant outflow, including defense signaling.

### Nutrient cycling

Plants are nutrient pumps. They draw mineral nutrients (N, P, K, etc.) from soil via roots and concentrate them in biomass. When that biomass dies or is consumed, nutrients re-enter the soil through decomposition. This cycling creates a feedback: plant growth depletes local soil nutrients, litterfall returns them, and the rate of decomposition determines how quickly they become available again. In nutrient-poor systems, plants with longer-lived, well-defended leaves (slow nutrient return) can reinforce nutrient poverty — a positive feedback loop that maintains ecosystem state (Hobbie 1992, Chapin et al. 2011).

### Trophic efficiency

Lindeman's (1942) trophic efficiency principle applies: only ~10% of energy at one trophic level transfers to the next. For plants, this means that the vast majority of the energy they fix is dissipated through respiration and decomposition rather than passed to herbivores. The trophic pyramid's shape — broad base of producers, narrow apex of top predators — is a direct consequence of this thermodynamic constraint applied iteratively.

## Feedback loops

Plants participate in numerous feedback loops that shape ecosystem dynamics:

### Reinforcing (positive) feedbacks

**Growth-shading feedback.** A plant that grows taller intercepts more light, gains more carbon, and can grow taller still. Meanwhile, shorter neighbors receive less light and grow more slowly or die. This is a classic winner-take-more dynamic that drives competitive exclusion in closed canopies and produces the vertical stratification of forests (Pacala et al. 1996).

**Litter-soil feedback.** Plant litter chemistry affects decomposition rates and soil nutrient availability, which in turn affects which plant species can establish. Species with recalcitrant litter (high lignin, low N) slow decomposition, creating nutrient-poor soils that favor more slow-growing, recalcitrant-litter species — a self-reinforcing loop (Hobbie 1992, Wardle et al. 2004). This feedback can maintain alternative stable states: nutrient-rich grassland vs. nutrient-poor heathland on the same substrate.

**Fire-vegetation feedback.** Grasses cure to highly flammable fuel, promoting fire, which kills woody seedlings and maintains the grassland state. Conversely, closed-canopy forest suppresses grass, reduces fire, and maintains the forest state. The savanna-forest boundary is maintained by this bistable feedback (Staver, Archibald & Levin 2011).

**Facilitation cascades.** Pioneer plants colonize bare substrate, modify conditions (shade, soil stability, organic matter), enabling establishment of later-successional species. This is the classical relay floristics model of succession (Connell & Slatyer 1977), where each stage facilitates the next — a reinforcing loop that drives directional change until a stable state is reached.

### Balancing (negative) feedbacks

**Density-dependent mortality.** As plant density increases, per-capita resource availability declines, increasing mortality. Yoda's -3/2 self-thinning law (Yoda et al. 1963) describes the resulting relationship between density and individual biomass in crowded stands — a classic negative feedback maintaining population within resource limits.

**Plant-herbivore regulation.** More plant biomass supports more herbivores, whose consumption reduces plant biomass, which reduces herbivore populations. This is the basic Lotka-Volterra consumer-resource dynamic, though in plant-herbivore systems the plant side has complications: compensatory regrowth, induced defenses, and spatial refugia all modify the coupling (Crawley 1983).

**Nutrient depletion-limitation.** Plant growth draws down soil nutrients, eventually limiting further growth until decomposition replenishes the pool. This creates oscillatory or steady-state dynamics depending on turnover rates.

### Cross-scale feedbacks

**Vegetation-climate coupling.** At landscape to global scales, vegetation affects albedo, evapotranspiration, and surface roughness, modifying regional climate, which feeds back to affect vegetation. The Amazon rainforest generates roughly 25-50% of its own rainfall through transpiration (Salati & Vose 1984). Deforestation can trigger a tipping point where reduced rainfall prevents forest recovery — a reinforcing feedback toward a degraded state.

## Key strategies

### Light competition

Light is the master resource for plants, and competition for it has a distinctive spatial structure. Unlike soil nutrients (which diffuse), light arrives directionally from above. This creates **asymmetric competition**: tall plants shade short ones, but not vice versa (Weiner 1990). The consequences:

- **Height growth as an arms race.** Trees invest enormous carbon in non-photosynthetic wood solely to position leaves above competitors. This is a tragedy-of-the-commons dynamic — all individuals would benefit from being shorter, but no individual can afford to be shorter than its neighbors (Falster & Westoby 2003).
- **Canopy stratification.** Forests self-organize into vertical layers: emergent trees, main canopy, understory, shrub layer, ground layer. Each layer represents a different light environment and selects for different trait syndromes.
- **Gap dynamics.** When a canopy tree dies, it creates a light gap — a pulse of resources that triggers intense competition among suppressed individuals and new recruits. Gap dynamics are the fundamental driver of forest community structure and diversity (Whitmore 1989).

### Defense and tolerance

Sessile organisms cannot flee herbivores. Plants have evolved two broad responses:

**Resistance (defense).** Reduce the amount of tissue lost. Mechanisms include physical defenses (thorns, trichomes, tough leaves), chemical defenses (alkaloids, tannins, terpenes), and biotic defenses (ant-plant mutualisms, volatile signals attracting herbivore predators). Defense is costly — carbon and nutrients invested in defense cannot be invested in growth or reproduction. This creates a **growth-defense tradeoff** that is central to plant ecological strategy theory (Coley, Bryant & Chapin 1985, Herms & Mattson 1992).

**Tolerance.** Maintain fitness despite tissue loss. Compensatory growth after herbivory, storage reserves that buffer against damage, meristems protected from grazers (grasses grow from the base, not the tip). Tolerance is favored in high-resource environments where regrowth is cheap; defense is favored in low-resource environments where lost tissue is expensive to replace (Coley et al. 1985).

From a flows perspective, defense reduces outflow to herbivores at the cost of reduced growth rate. Tolerance maintains growth rate despite higher outflow. Both strategies modify the same flow — biomass to herbivores — but through different mechanisms.

### Dispersal

Seeds are the spatial flow mechanism for sessile organisms. Dispersal kernels — the probability distribution of dispersal distance — determine how plant populations spread, colonize gaps, and maintain metapopulation connectivity. Key modes:

- **Wind (anemochory)**: Light seeds, long tails on dispersal kernels. Good for colonizing distant gaps. Low precision.
- **Animal (zoochory)**: Fruit rewards in exchange for transport. Directed dispersal to suitable microsites (e.g., birds deposit seeds under perches). Higher precision, higher cost.
- **Ballistic (autochory)**: Self-powered ejection. Short range, no dependency on vectors.
- **Gravity (barochory)**: Heavy seeds fall near parent. Concentrates offspring near proven microsites but intensifies parent-offspring competition.

The shape of the dispersal kernel has profound consequences for spatial pattern formation, species coexistence, and invasion dynamics (Clark et al. 1999, Nathan & Muller-Landau 2000).

### Reproductive strategies

The r/K continuum (MacArthur & Wilson 1967, Pianka 1970) manifests clearly in plants:

- **r-strategy (ruderal)**: Many small seeds, minimal per-offspring investment, rapid maturation, short lifespan. Colonizers of disturbed or ephemeral habitats. Annual weeds are the archetype.
- **K-strategy (competitive)**: Few large seeds, high per-offspring investment (endosperm reserves, seed defenses), slow maturation, long lifespan. Competitors in stable, resource-limited environments. Canopy trees are the archetype.

Grime's (1977) CSR triangle adds a third axis — stress tolerance — distinguishing competitive (C), stress-tolerant (S), and ruderal (R) strategies. This captures the observation that some plants succeed not by competing or colonizing but by persisting where others cannot.

From an energy-flow perspective, reproductive investment represents a diversion of fixed carbon from somatic growth and maintenance into propagule production. The timing, magnitude, and frequency of this diversion (semelparity vs. iteroparity, mast seeding vs. annual reproduction) shape population dynamics and interactions with seed predators and dispersers.

## The sessile constraint

Sessility is the defining systemic feature of plants. A plant cannot relocate in response to changing conditions. Every strategy it employs must operate within this constraint. The consequences are pervasive:

**Resource acquisition is local.** A plant can only access the light, water, and nutrients available at its rooted position. This makes spatial location the most important "decision" in a plant's life — and it is made once, at germination, often randomly. Local resource heterogeneity becomes the dominant structuring force.

**Competition is a war of attrition.** Two competing plants cannot choose to avoid each other. They compete until one dies or one outgrows the other. Competition is slow, persistent, and spatially explicit. This contrasts with mobile organisms, which can partition space through behavioral avoidance.

**Defense must be passive.** Without flight, plants must either deter herbivores (chemical/physical defense), tolerate damage, or rely on neighbors for associational resistance. This creates strong selection for investment in defense — a cost that mobile organisms can partially avoid through escape behavior.

**Reproduction must include dispersal.** A sessile organism that merely buds offspring at its own location condemns them to compete with itself. Seeds, spores, and vegetative runners are solutions to the problem of dispersing progeny away from the parent, where resources may be less depleted and enemy pressure (Janzen-Connell effect) less intense (Janzen 1970, Connell 1971).

**Environmental modification compounds over time.** Because plants cannot leave, they are subject to — and benefit from — the environmental changes they create. Soil modification, microclimate creation, and litter accumulation all feed back onto the plant that caused them. This makes plants potent ecosystem engineers. A mobile organism moves through environments; a sessile organism transforms its environment.

**Information acquisition is constrained.** Plants sense their environment (light quality via phytochromes, neighbor proximity via far-red reflection, gravity, touch) but cannot actively explore. Their sensing is passive and local, driven by the signals that reach them rather than by movement toward signals.

**Temporal strategy replaces spatial strategy.** Where mobile organisms respond to environmental change by moving, plants respond by waiting — dormancy, seed banks, phenological timing. The relevant timescale for plant strategy is often seasonal to decadal, not moment-to-moment.

The sessile-mobile divide is not binary in nature (clonal plants spread slowly, some marine invertebrates are sessile) but it is one of the deepest strategic divides in ecology. It defines a fundamentally different life strategy: one that modifies the local environment rather than selecting among environments.

## ABM and computational literature

Agent-based and individual-based models (IBMs) have a long history in plant ecology, arguably longer than in animal ecology, because the sessile nature of plants makes spatially explicit individual-based approaches natural.

### Forest gap models

The founding lineage of plant IBMs. These models simulate forest dynamics by tracking individual trees competing for light in spatial gaps.

- **JABOWA** (Botkin, Janak & Wallis 1972): The first forest gap model. Simulates individual trees on small plots, with growth limited by light, temperature, and soil. Demonstrated that successional dynamics and species composition could emerge from individual-level growth rules. Foundational for all subsequent gap models.
- **FORET/ZELIG** (Shugart 1984, Urban & Shugart 1992): Extended JABOWA with environmental gradients and landscape-scale dynamics. Shugart's (1984) *A Theory of Forest Dynamics* is the key reference for the conceptual framework.
- **SORTIE** (Pacala et al. 1996): Spatially explicit gap model with individual tree positions. Parameterized from field data on light-dependent growth, mortality, and recruitment. Demonstrated that asymmetric light competition alone could reproduce observed forest composition patterns. Key insight: neighborhood interactions, not plot-level averages, drive dynamics.
- **FORMIND** (Kohler & Huth 1998, Fischer et al. 2016): Individual-based model for tropical forests. Simulates trees on a grid of patches with explicit light competition, carbon balance, and gap formation. Widely used for tropical forest dynamics and logging scenarios.
- **iLand** (Seidl et al. 2012): Process-based forest landscape model combining individual tree dynamics with landscape-scale disturbance (fire, wind, bark beetles). Represents the current state of the art in spatially explicit forest IBMs.

### Grassland and general vegetation models

- **Coffin & Lauenroth (1990)**: Early individual-based model of semi-arid grassland dynamics, simulating establishment, growth, and mortality of individual grass and shrub plants on a grid. Demonstrated that spatial competition patterns reproduce observed zonation.
- **Jeltsch et al. (1996)**: Grid-based model of bush encroachment in savannas. Individual-level rules for tree and grass competition, fire, and grazing produce landscape-scale pattern shifts.
- **Grimm & Railsback (2005)**: *Individual-based Modeling and Ecology* provides the theoretical and methodological framework for plant IBMs. The ODD (Overview, Design concepts, Details) protocol they introduced (Grimm et al. 2006, 2010) is now standard for describing plant (and all) IBMs.

### NetLogo community models

The NetLogo platform (Wilensky 1999) hosts several relevant plant community models:

- **Fire** model (in the NetLogo Models Library): Simple model of fire spread through a forest, demonstrating percolation thresholds.
- **Sunflower Biomorphs** and **Plant Growth** models demonstrate L-system and resource-based plant growth.
- **Wolf Sheep Predation** (Wilensky 1997): Though animal-focused, includes a grass regrowth layer that demonstrates producer-consumer coupling.
- Various user-contributed models of forest succession, seed dispersal, and plant competition are available in the NetLogo community repository.

### Plant competition and spatial pattern models

- **Berger & Hildenbrandt (2000)**: IBM of mangrove forest dynamics. Zone-of-influence approach where each tree competes for light within a circular crown area. Demonstrated self-organization of spatial patterns from simple competition rules.
- **Grimm et al. (1999)**: Individual-based beech forest model showing how gap dynamics and light competition produce realistic stand structure.
- **Tilman's (1994)** competition-colonization tradeoff model: Not strictly an ABM, but the spatial competition framework (species coexist when good competitors are poor colonizers and vice versa) has been widely implemented in agent-based form.
- **Scanlon et al. (2007)**: Spatial model of vegetation pattern formation in arid systems, showing how local facilitation and long-range competition for water produce spots, stripes, and gaps — a model of self-organized spatial patterning relevant to sessile agent systems.

### Key modeling principles from the literature

Several principles recur across the plant IBM literature:

1. **Asymmetric competition matters.** Symmetric (equal sharing) vs. asymmetric (size-proportional) competition rules produce dramatically different dynamics (Weiner 1990, Schwinning & Weiner 1998). Light competition is asymmetric; belowground competition is closer to symmetric.
2. **Individual variation drives population dynamics.** Even small individual-level differences in initial size or position produce major divergence over time through competitive amplification (Huston & DeAngelis 1994).
3. **Spatial explicitness is essential.** Mean-field (well-mixed) models fail to capture plant community dynamics because neighborhood effects dominate. Plant models must be spatially explicit (DeAngelis & Mooij 2005).
4. **Allometric scaling simplifies parameterization.** Many plant traits scale predictably with body size following power laws (West, Brown & Enquist 1999). A diameter or biomass variable can generate height, crown area, metabolic rate, and growth rate through allometric equations, reducing free parameters.

## System without plants

Remove primary producers from a terrestrial ecosystem and the system collapses structurally, not just quantitatively.

**Energy flow ceases.** Solar flux no longer converts to biologically available energy. The sole remaining energy stock is whatever biomass existed at the moment producers were removed — a finite, non-renewing pool. Consumers deplete this stock on a timescale proportional to their metabolic rates. Small-bodied, high-metabolism organisms die first. Large-bodied organisms persist longer but face the same inevitable starvation.

**The detrital pulse.** Immediately after producer removal, the dead plant biomass becomes a massive detrital resource. Decomposers experience a temporary boom as the stored energy in plant material becomes available. But this is a one-time liquidation of capital, not a sustainable income. The decomposer boom is followed by a decomposer crash when the detrital stock is exhausted.

**Trophic cascade upward.** Herbivores lose their food source first. Their die-off removes the prey base for their predators. The collapse propagates up the trophic pyramid with a time lag at each level.

**Physical habitat loss.** Without plants, soil destabilizes (no root binding), microclimate buffering disappears (no canopy shade, no transpirational cooling), and the physical structure that defines niches — canopy layers, root zone, litter layer — ceases to exist. Even organisms that do not eat plants lose their habitat.

**Nutrient cycling halts.** The plant-driven nutrient pump stops. Remaining soil nutrients are either leached away by water (no root uptake to retain them) or locked in recalcitrant organic matter with no decomposition inputs.

The timescale of collapse depends on the ecosystem. A grassland (low standing biomass, fast turnover) collapses in weeks to months. A forest (massive standing biomass, slow turnover) takes years to decades as the wood stock slowly decomposes. But the trajectory is the same: monotonic decline toward a system with no biologically available energy and no physical structure. What remains is rock, water, and atmosphere — the abiotic template that existed before life colonized.

This is the fundamental reason primary producers are non-negotiable. They are the tap. Without them, the system is a bathtub draining with no faucet.
