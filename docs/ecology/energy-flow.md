# Energy Flow

Energy is the master currency of ecology. Matter cycles — carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus circulate indefinitely through organisms, dead pools, and abiotic reservoirs (see [nutrient cycling](nutrient-cycling.md)). Energy does not cycle. It flows in one direction: from solar radiation through living systems to heat, degraded and dissipated at every step. This asymmetry between matter (conserved, recycled) and energy (conserved globally, degraded locally) is the thermodynamic foundation on which all ecological structure rests.

## Thermodynamic constraints

### The two laws in ecology

The first law of thermodynamics — conservation of energy — holds globally. Energy entering an ecosystem as solar radiation must equal energy leaving as heat plus any change in stored energy (biomass, dead organic matter). Over long timescales, inputs and outputs balance: ecosystems do not create or destroy energy.

The second law — entropy increases in every real process — is where ecology gets its structure. Every energy conversion is lossy. Photosynthesis converts roughly 1–2% of incident solar radiation to chemical energy (Zhu, Long & Ort 2010). Every subsequent transfer — consumption, assimilation, growth, reproduction — dissipates a fraction as heat. These are not inefficiencies to be engineered away; they are physical necessities. No real process converts energy at 100% efficiency because doing so would require zero entropy production, which the second law forbids.

The consequence is directional: energy flows from concentrated (solar photons, chemical bonds) to dispersed (metabolic heat). Living systems sit in this flow. They do not reverse it — they exploit it temporarily, building local order at the cost of increasing total entropy.

### Ecosystems as dissipative structures

Schneider & Kay (1994, 1995) argued that ecosystems are dissipative structures in Prigogine's sense — ordered, far-from-equilibrium systems maintained by continuous energy throughput. Their core claim: the second law does not merely permit complex living systems but actively drives their emergence. A temperature gradient (solar input against the cold of space) spontaneously generates organised structures that degrade the gradient more effectively than the unorganised state would. A mature forest dissipates more incoming solar energy (as evapotranspiration, longwave radiation, metabolic heat) than bare ground — its complexity is thermodynamically favoured because it is a more effective gradient-degrading machine.

Empirical support: forests maintain substantially cooler canopy temperatures than adjacent clearings or agricultural fields (Schneider & Kay 1995). The forest is doing more thermodynamic work — transpiring water, cycling nutrients, building and maintaining biomass — and all that work degrades the solar energy gradient more completely.

This framing inverts the intuition that complexity is fragile and improbable. In a system driven by a sustained energy gradient, complexity is the expected outcome — the second law favours it because structured dissipation outperforms unstructured dissipation.

### The efficiency-power trade-off

There is a fundamental thermodynamic tension between efficiency and power (rate of energy use). Maximum efficiency (approaching Carnot efficiency) corresponds to zero power output — the process runs infinitely slowly. Maximum power corresponds to less than maximum efficiency — the process runs fast but wastes more. Lotka (1922) first noted that natural selection should favour maximum power rather than maximum efficiency, because organisms that process energy faster can acquire more resources and reproduce more, even if they waste more per unit acquired. Odum (1983) formalised this as the Maximum Power Principle: systems that maximise useful power throughput are competitively favoured over systems that maximise efficiency.

The implication for ecology: organisms and ecosystems do not minimise waste. They maximise throughput. A cheetah's sprint is metabolically catastrophic — energy expenditure at 30–40x basal rate for seconds — but it captures prey. A tree's canopy intercepts far more light than its photosynthetic machinery can use at any instant, but the excess interception shades competitors. These are power strategies, not efficiency strategies.

## Temporal structure of energy input

Solar flux is the tap, but it is not constant. It varies on three timescales, and organisms have evolved to track all three.

### Diel cycles

The day-night cycle is the most fundamental temporal structure in the energy input. Photosynthesis is a daytime process — producers fix carbon only when light is available. This creates a daily pulse of energy input followed by a period of zero input during which organisms continue to respire, drawing down reserves accumulated during the day.

Nearly all organisms have endogenous circadian clocks entrained to the light-dark cycle (Dunlap 1999). These clocks coordinate activity, metabolism, gene expression, and physiology with the predictable daily alternation of energy availability and risk. Diurnal organisms forage during light hours; nocturnal organisms exploit reduced predation pressure and competition at night. Diel vertical migration in aquatic systems — zooplankton ascending to feed in productive surface waters at night and descending to avoid visual predators by day (Hays 2003) — is one of the largest daily biomass movements on Earth, driven entirely by the diel light cycle.

The ecological consequence is temporal partitioning: the same spatial habitat supports different communities by day and night, increasing total species coexistence beyond what a constant-light environment would allow.

### Seasonal cycles

The tilt of Earth's axis creates predictable annual variation in day length and solar angle, producing seasons. The ecological responses are pervasive:

**Phenology.** The timing of biological events — leaf-out, flowering, fruiting, migration, hibernation, breeding — is cued primarily by photoperiod (day length) and temperature, both of which track the seasonal energy cycle. Phenological mismatches — where interacting species respond to different cues and fall out of synchrony — can disrupt trophic links (Visser & Both 2005). Pollinator emergence before flower bloom, or caterpillar peak before bird chick-rearing, decouples energy flows that depend on temporal coincidence.

**Dormancy and storage.** Organisms in seasonal environments must bridge periods of zero or negative energy balance. Deciduous trees shed leaves (eliminating photosynthetic income but also eliminating maintenance cost of leaves in winter). Animals hibernate, reducing metabolic rate by 90–95% (Geiser 2004). Seeds and spores persist through unfavourable seasons as metabolically inert propagules. These are all strategies for surviving a predictable interruption of the energy tap.

**Migration.** Large-scale seasonal movement tracks the spatial shifting of energy availability. Wildebeest follow the rains across the Serengeti. Arctic terns breed at high latitudes in the northern summer and overwinter at high latitudes in the southern summer, exploiting two energy peaks per year. Migration is energetically expensive but allows organisms to avoid the metabolic cost of dormancy by relocating to where the tap is still flowing.

### Stochastic variation

Superimposed on the predictable diel and seasonal cycles is stochastic variation — weather. Cloud cover, storms, drought, and temperature anomalies create unpredictable fluctuation in energy input on timescales of hours to years. This variation is not cyclic and cannot be anticipated by circadian or seasonal clocks.

Organisms cope with stochastic variation through reserves (fat stores, starch reserves, DEB theory's reserve pool), behavioural flexibility (opportunistic foraging, facultative dormancy), and bet-hedging strategies (see [life history theory](life-history-theory.md)). At the population and ecosystem level, stochastic energy variation contributes to disturbance dynamics — drought-induced mortality, storm damage, fire ignition — covered in [disturbance and succession](disturbance-and-succession.md).

## Metabolic scaling

### Kleiber's law

Max Kleiber (1932) established empirically that basal metabolic rate scales with body mass as B ∝ M^(3/4). This three-quarter power law holds across roughly 20 orders of magnitude in body mass, from bacteria to whales. It is one of the most robust quantitative relationships in biology.

The exponent matters. A 2/3 exponent would follow from surface-area-to-volume scaling (heat dissipation through body surface). The observed 3/4 exponent is steeper — large organisms have higher metabolic rates than surface-area scaling predicts, though still lower per unit mass than small organisms.

### The metabolic theory of ecology

West, Brown & Enquist (1997) derived the 3/4 exponent from first principles. Their model rests on three assumptions: (1) the internal transport network (circulatory system, vascular system) is a space-filling fractal that branches hierarchically to reach all cells; (2) the terminal units of the network (capillaries, leaf petioles) are size-invariant; (3) the network minimises the energy required to distribute resources. From these constraints, the 3/4 scaling emerges as a geometric necessity of supplying a three-dimensional body through a branching network.

Brown, Gillooly, Allen, Savage & West (2004) unified metabolic scaling with temperature dependence into a single equation:

B = B₀ × M^(3/4) × e^(−E/kT)

where E is the activation energy of metabolism (~0.6–0.7 eV), k is Boltzmann's constant, and T is absolute temperature. This equation predicts a cascade of quarter-power scalings across biological levels:

- Mass-specific metabolic rate, heart rate, respiratory rate: ∝ M^(−1/4)
- Lifespan, developmental time, blood circulation time: ∝ M^(+1/4)
- Population density: ∝ M^(−3/4)
- Home range area: ∝ M^(+1)
- Population growth rate: ∝ M^(−1/4)

The MTE claims that metabolism — constrained by body size and temperature — is the fundamental rate that sets the pace of nearly all biological processes, from cellular biochemistry to ecosystem production.

### Criticisms and limits

The universality of the 3/4 exponent is contested. Kozłowski & Konarzewski (2004) argued that the WBE derivation's assumptions are too restrictive and that multiple different mechanisms could produce the same prediction, making the model underdetermined. Empirical analyses find that the exponent varies by taxon, life stage, and physiological state — it is not a universal constant but a central tendency with meaningful variation (Price et al. 2012). The MTE successfully predicts broad cross-species patterns but does not explain the large, systematic residual variation within taxa.

The theory is most powerful as a first-order constraint: given body size and temperature, metabolic rate is predictable within an order of magnitude. Finer predictions require taxon-specific biology.

## Individual energy budgets

### Dynamic Energy Budget theory

Kooijman's Dynamic Energy Budget (DEB) theory (1979; synthesised in Kooijman 2010) describes how an individual organism acquires energy and allocates it across its life. DEB is the most complete individual-level energetics framework in ecology, with parameters estimated for over 1,000 animal species (Marques et al. 2018).

The core structure:

1. **Assimilation.** Food is converted to energy and enters a reserve pool. Assimilation rate scales with body surface area (M^(2/3)) because food uptake is a surface process — gut lining, root surface, gill area.

2. **Reserve mobilisation.** Energy flows from the reserve to fund all other processes. The reserve acts as a buffer between variable food intake and steady metabolic demand.

3. **The κ-rule.** A fixed fraction κ of mobilised reserve is allocated to somatic maintenance plus growth. The remaining fraction (1−κ) goes to maturation (in juveniles) or reproduction (in adults). This simple partition generates the core life-history trade-off: more to soma means less to reproduction, and vice versa.

4. **Maintenance priority.** Within each branch, maintenance takes precedence over growth or reproduction. If reserves are insufficient, growth stops but maintenance continues. Under severe starvation, the organism catabolises its own structure to fund maintenance. Death occurs when structure is depleted below a critical threshold.

The consequences are powerful. Because assimilation scales with surface area but maintenance scales with volume, an organism's growth rate necessarily decelerates as it gets larger — maintenance claims an increasing share of the energy budget. This produces the von Bertalanffy growth curve as an emergent prediction rather than a fitted function. DEB also predicts how starvation affects body composition (reserves are mobilised before structure), how reproduction rate depends on food availability and body size, and how organisms at different life stages respond differently to the same environmental conditions.

### Scaling from individual to population

Kooijman & Metz (1984) demonstrated that population dynamics of *Daphnia* could be predicted from individual-level DEB parameters alone — no population-level parameters needed. Individual energy budgets determine growth rates, reproduction rates, and mortality schedules; aggregate these across a population and the population dynamics follow. This established DEB as a bridge between physiological ecology and population ecology (reviewed by Nisbet et al. 2010).

## Trophic transfer

### Lindeman's trophic-dynamic concept

Raymond Lindeman's (1942) "The Trophic-Dynamic Aspect of Ecology" was the first rigorous quantification of energy flow through an ecosystem. Working on Cedar Bog Lake, Minnesota, Lindeman measured energy stocks at successive trophic levels and calculated the efficiency of transfer between them. His calculated efficiencies ranged from 0.1% to 37.5% across different transfers, with a central tendency around 10–20%.

The "10% rule" — that roughly 10% of energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next — is a pedagogical shorthand, not a law. Lindeman himself reported wide variation.

### Components of transfer efficiency

Since Lindeman, ecologists have decomposed trophic transfer into component efficiencies:

**Assimilation efficiency (AE):** the fraction of ingested energy actually absorbed across the gut wall. The remainder is egested as faeces and enters the detrital pathway. AE varies systematically by diet:
- Carnivores: 60–90%. Animal tissue is chemically similar to consumer tissue — high protein and fat, little structural carbohydrate.
- Herbivores: 15–80%. Enormous variation depending on food type (fruits 60–80%, leaves 30–60%, woody material 15–30%) and digestive strategy (ruminant fermentation achieves higher efficiency from cellulose than monogastric digestion).
- Detritivores: highly variable, depending on substrate quality (labile dissolved organic matter vs. recalcitrant lignocellulose).

**Net production efficiency (NPE):** the fraction of assimilated energy that becomes new biomass. The remainder is respired — used for maintenance, activity, thermoregulation, and dissipated as heat.
- Ectotherms (fish, insects, reptiles): 25–75%. Basal metabolic costs are low; most assimilated energy can go to growth.
- Endotherms (birds, mammals): rarely exceeds 5%. The overwhelming majority of assimilated energy is lost as heat in thermoregulation. This single factor — endothermy — is the largest driver of low trophic efficiency in endotherm-dominated food chains.

**Ecological (trophic) efficiency:** production at trophic level n+1 divided by production at level n. This is the quantity Lindeman measured — the compound of assimilation efficiency, net production efficiency, and the fraction of production at level n that is actually consumed (rather than entering the detrital pathway). Measured values range from approximately 5% to 20% (Welch 1968), with aquatic systems generally higher than terrestrial ones because aquatic food chains are dominated by ectotherms.

### Why food chains are short

The compounding effect of ~10% efficiency at each step limits most food chains to 4–5 trophic levels. At 10% per step, the energy available at the fifth level is 0.01% of primary production — a vanishing resource base. This is a thermodynamic ceiling, not a biological one. Aquatic systems occasionally sustain longer food chains because their higher per-step efficiency (ectotherm-dominated, higher assimilation of protein-rich phytoplankton) provides a larger energy base at upper levels.

### The metabolic tax of body temperature

The difference between ectotherm and endotherm net production efficiency is stark and structurally important. An endothermic predator (wolf, hawk) converts roughly 1–5% of its assimilated energy to new biomass. An ectothermic predator (bass, spider) converts 25–50%. The energy "lost" to thermoregulation in endotherms is not wasted in a fitness sense — it buys activity independence from environmental temperature, enabling sustained pursuit predation, nocturnal foraging, and exploitation of cold environments. But thermodynamically, it is dissipated heat that could otherwise have become offspring.

This trade-off — between metabolic independence and metabolic efficiency — is one of the deepest in animal ecology. It shapes food chain length, trophic pyramid shape, and the relative biomass of predators and prey across biomes.

## Ecosystem metabolism

### Production, respiration, and balance

At the ecosystem level, energy flow is described by aggregate terms:

- **Gross primary production (GPP):** total carbon fixed by photosynthesis.
- **Autotrophic respiration (Ra):** respiratory costs of the producers themselves — maintenance, growth, reproduction. Typically 40–70% of GPP in terrestrial systems (Gifford 2003). Remarkably stable across biomes.
- **Net primary production (NPP):** GPP − Ra. The organic matter actually available to the rest of the ecosystem.
- **Heterotrophic respiration (Rh):** respiration by consumers and decomposers.
- **Net ecosystem production (NEP):** NPP − Rh = GPP − Ra − Rh. The net rate of carbon (and energy) accumulation or loss.
- **Ecosystem respiration (ER):** Ra + Rh. The total respiratory flux.

### The P/R trajectory

Odum (1969) proposed that the ratio of production to respiration (P/R) is the energetic signature of successional stage:

- **Early succession:** P > R (P/R > 1). The system is accumulating biomass and organic matter. Production outpaces the respiratory cost of maintaining what exists. Pioneer communities are net energy accumulators.
- **Mature systems:** P ≈ R (P/R → 1). Production approximately balances total respiration. The system is in energetic steady state — it is using its entire energy income to maintain existing structure. The ratio of standing biomass to production (B/P) rises continuously during succession, indicating increasing structural complexity per unit energy throughput.
- **After disturbance:** P/R resets above 1. The system re-enters accumulation phase.

Odum's key insight: mature ecosystems use their energy income to maintain complexity rather than to produce additional biomass. The B/P ratio — how much structure is maintained per unit of energy flow — is the thermodynamic measure of ecosystem maturity.

### Refinements to Odum

Later empirical work has refined the mechanism. Odum predicted that autotrophic respiration increases continuously with stand age until it matches GPP. Empirical data on boreal forests (Goulden et al. 2011) found that both GPP and Ra decline in aging stands, but GPP declines faster — NPP drops not because maintenance costs rise absolutely but because photosynthetic capacity declines in old trees. NEP approaches zero in old-growth not because Ra rises to meet GPP but because GPP declines toward the Ra baseline (Ryan et al. 2014). The directional outcome is the same (P/R → 1 with maturity), but the mechanism is GPP declining rather than Ra rising.

### The two pathways

Energy leaving producers follows two routes (see also [nutrient cycling](nutrient-cycling.md)):

**The grazer chain.** Herbivores consume living tissue and pass energy up to predators. In most terrestrial ecosystems, the grazer chain processes only 5–15% of NPP (Cyr & Pace 1993). The vast majority of plant production is not eaten alive.

**The detrital chain.** Dead organic matter — litterfall, root turnover, carcasses, faeces — enters the dead organic matter pool and is processed by decomposers. In terrestrial systems, 80–90% of NPP follows this route. The detrital chain is the dominant energy pathway on land.

Aquatic systems differ markedly: phytoplankton have little structural tissue, turn over rapidly, and are consumed efficiently. The grazer chain processes 40–80% of marine NPP (Cebrian 1999). This inversion — from detritus-dominated on land to grazer-dominated in water — reflects the fundamental difference between large, long-lived, structurally defended primary producers (trees) and small, short-lived, undefended ones (phytoplankton).

### Energy storage and residence times

Energy resides in ecosystem stocks for characteristic durations:

| Stock | Residence time |
|---|---|
| Phytoplankton biomass | Days to weeks |
| Leaf biomass | Months to 1 year |
| Wood biomass | Decades to centuries |
| Soil organic matter (labile) | Months to years |
| Soil organic matter (humus) | Decades to millennia |

The slow stocks — wood and humus — are quantitatively dominant. They represent accumulated production from long periods of P > R. Their decomposition rate is the primary control on long-term energy cycling: temperature, moisture, and substrate chemistry (lignin:nitrogen ratio) regulate how fast this stored energy is released (Lloyd & Taylor 1994).

## Feedback loops

**Metabolic rate–body size feedback.** Larger organisms have lower mass-specific metabolic rates (Kleiber 1932). This means larger organisms need less energy per unit mass to survive but process resources more slowly per unit mass. The consequence is a size-related trade-off: large organisms are energetically efficient per gram but slow to reproduce; small organisms are metabolically expensive per gram but fast to reproduce. This scales up to determine the relative abundance and turnover rates of different-sized organisms in an ecosystem.

**Production–respiration feedback.** As ecosystems accumulate biomass during succession, total respiration increases (more structure to maintain). Eventually respiration approaches production and net accumulation slows. This is a negative feedback: the more complex the system becomes, the harder it is to become more complex, because maintenance costs consume an increasing share of the energy budget (Odum 1969).

**Trophic efficiency feedback.** Energy loss at each trophic transfer creates a natural limit on food chain length and predator biomass. More trophic levels mean more cumulative loss, meaning less energy available at the top. This constrains the elaboration of food webs — adding trophic levels requires sufficient energy at the base to sustain the compounded losses.

**Decomposition–production coupling.** Decomposition releases nutrients locked in dead organic matter, making them available for producer uptake and new production. The rate of decomposition therefore controls the rate at which the production-sustaining nutrient pool is replenished. Slow decomposition (cold, dry, or chemically recalcitrant substrates) means slow nutrient return, meaning lower production, meaning less litter input, meaning even slower decomposition — a positive feedback that can lock systems into low-productivity states. Fast decomposition creates the reverse: rapid nutrient cycling supporting high production.

**The endothermy tax as a flow regulator.** Endothermic consumers dissipate most of their assimilated energy as heat. This massive energy drain reduces the biomass of endothermic predators relative to their prey, enforces steep trophic pyramids, and limits food chain length in endotherm-dominated systems. The same tax buys activity independence, enabling sustained pursuit and year-round foraging — which in turn affects prey population dynamics and spatial patterns.
