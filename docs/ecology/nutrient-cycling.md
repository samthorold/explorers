# Nutrient Cycling

Ecosystems run on the continuous circulation of matter through living organisms, dead organic pools, and abiotic reservoirs. Where the taxon docs in this series describe the organisms that move nutrients, this document describes the cycling itself — the stocks, flows, residence times, and feedback loops that govern how carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus circulate as a complete system. The critical insight is that real ecosystems do not have one currency but several, and their dynamics often arise from the interactions between currencies rather than the behavior of any single one.

## Carbon cycle as energy flow

Carbon is the backbone of biological energy accounting. Solar radiation is captured by producers and converted into organic carbon compounds; that carbon then flows through the ecosystem and is ultimately returned to the atmosphere as CO2 through respiration. The total flow can be described in three aggregate quantities:

- **Gross primary productivity (GPP)**: total carbon fixed by photosynthesis. Globally, terrestrial GPP is approximately 120 Pg C/year (Beer et al. 2010), with a comparable amount fixed in marine systems.
- **Autotrophic respiration (Ra)**: roughly 50% of GPP is consumed by the producers themselves for maintenance, growth, and reproduction. This fraction is remarkably stable across biomes (Gifford 2003).
- **Net primary productivity (NPP)**: GPP minus Ra. This is the energy actually available to the rest of the ecosystem — the flow rate into the biotic economy.

### The two pathways

Lindeman (1942) identified two channels through which NPP flows:

**The grazer chain.** Herbivores consume living plant tissue and pass energy up to predators. In most terrestrial ecosystems, the grazer chain processes only 5-15% of NPP (Cyr & Pace 1993). Aquatic systems are different — phytoplankton have little structural tissue and are consumed rapidly, so the grazer chain can process 40-80% of marine NPP (Cebrian 1999).

**The detrital chain.** Dead organic matter — litterfall, root turnover, animal carcasses, feces — enters the dead organic matter (DOM) pool and is processed by decomposers (fungi and bacteria). In terrestrial systems, 80-90% of NPP follows this route. The detrital chain is therefore the dominant energy pathway on land.

### Stocks and residence times

Carbon resides in three major ecosystem stocks, each with a characteristic turnover time:

| Stock | Size (terrestrial biosphere) | Residence time |
|---|---|---|
| Living biomass | ~450 Gt C (Bar-On et al. 2018) | Leaves: months-1 year; wood: decades-centuries |
| Dead organic matter (litter, soil organic matter) | ~1,500-2,400 Gt C in soil (Scharlemann et al. 2014) | Litter: months-years; humus: decades-millennia |
| Atmospheric CO2 | ~870 Gt C | ~4 years (exchange with biosphere) |

The slow stock — soil organic matter, especially humus — holds more carbon than the atmosphere and living biomass combined. Its decomposition rate is the dominant control on long-term carbon cycling. Temperature, moisture, and substrate chemistry (lignin:nitrogen ratio) regulate this rate. Decomposition roughly doubles for every 10 degrees C increase (Lloyd & Taylor 1994), creating a powerful climate-carbon feedback.

### Net ecosystem production

Net ecosystem production (NEP) is NPP minus heterotrophic respiration — the net rate of carbon accumulation or loss. Young, growing ecosystems tend to be carbon sinks (NEP > 0). Mature ecosystems approach carbon neutrality as decomposition catches up with production. Disturbance resets the clock. This framing (Odum 1969) means ecosystem carbon balance is a function of the relative timescales of production and decomposition, not of production alone.

## Nitrogen cycle

Nitrogen is the nutrient most often limiting terrestrial production (Vitousek & Howarth 1991) and the one with the most complex biogeochemical cycle, because it exists in multiple oxidation states and most transformations are mediated by specialized microbial groups.

### The cycle as a loop

The nitrogen cycle can be traced as a sequence of transformations, each with a characteristic microbial mediator:

1. **Fixation.** Atmospheric N2 is converted to biologically available ammonium (NH4+). This is the primary input to the biological cycle. Biological fixation is performed by diazotrophs — free-living soil bacteria (Azotobacter), symbiotic root-nodule bacteria (Rhizobium with legumes, Frankia with alders), and cyanobacteria. Global biological fixation is approximately 100-200 Tg N/year (Fowler et al. 2013). Lightning and industrial processes (Haber-Bosch) add a comparable anthropogenic input.

2. **Assimilation.** Plants and microbes take up NH4+ and NO3- from soil solution and incorporate nitrogen into organic molecules (amino acids, proteins, nucleic acids). This is the flow from the inorganic to the organic pool.

3. **Ammonification (mineralization).** When organisms die or excrete waste, decomposers break down organic nitrogen compounds and release NH4+ back to the soil. This is the return flow from organic to inorganic — the step that closes the loop for most nitrogen. Fungi and bacteria both mediate this step; its rate depends on the C:N ratio of the substrate. Material with C:N above ~25:1 causes net microbial immobilization (microbes absorb nitrogen rather than releasing it), slowing nitrogen availability (Chapin, Matson & Vitousek 2011).

4. **Nitrification.** Ammonium is oxidized to nitrite (NO2-) by Nitrosomonas-group bacteria, then to nitrate (NO3-) by Nitrobacter-group bacteria. Nitrification is a chemoautotrophic process — the microbes derive energy from the oxidation itself. Nitrate is highly mobile in soil solution (unlike ammonium, which binds to clay particles), so nitrification simultaneously makes nitrogen more available to plants and more vulnerable to leaching loss.

5. **Denitrification.** Under anaerobic conditions, facultative anaerobic bacteria (Pseudomonas, Bacillus) reduce nitrate back to N2 or N2O, returning nitrogen to the atmosphere. This is the primary loss pathway from the biological cycle. Denitrification is highest in waterlogged soils, wetlands, and aquatic sediments. It creates a coupling between oxygen availability and nitrogen retention.

### Nitrogen limitation

Nitrogen limits production across most terrestrial biomes (LeBauer & Treseder 2008). The mechanism is straightforward in stocks-and-flows terms: fixation is energetically expensive (~16 ATP per N2 molecule fixed), so the input rate is low relative to biological demand. Ecosystems that have sustained high fixation inputs (e.g., alder stands, legume-rich grasslands) eventually shift to phosphorus limitation as nitrogen saturates.

### N:P stoichiometry

The ratio of nitrogen to phosphorus in an ecosystem indicates which nutrient is more likely limiting. Terrestrial systems tend toward N-limitation (N:P of available nutrients is low relative to biological demand), while older, highly weathered tropical soils tend toward P-limitation (Walker & Syers 1976). Freshwater systems are classically P-limited (Schindler 1977), while marine systems show co-limitation or N-limitation depending on region and timescale.

## Phosphorus cycle

Phosphorus differs fundamentally from carbon and nitrogen in having no significant atmospheric phase. It enters ecosystems through the slow weathering of rock minerals (primarily aite) and cycles tightly through biological uptake, organic matter decomposition, and soil chemistry. There is no biological fixation equivalent — no organism can pull phosphorus from the air.

### Geological input, biological cycling

The phosphorus cycle operates on two timescales:

**Geological (slow).** Rock weathering releases phosphate (PO4 3-) into soil solution at rates of approximately 0.05-1 kg P/ha/year, depending on rock type and climate (Schlesinger & Bernhardt 2013). This is the ultimate input. Losses occur through erosion and leaching to waterways, eventually depositing phosphorus in ocean sediments. The return to land requires tectonic uplift — a process on the order of millions of years. This makes phosphorus a fundamentally non-renewable nutrient on ecological timescales.

**Biological (fast).** Once in the soil, phosphorus cycles rapidly between organic and inorganic pools through plant uptake, incorporation into biomass, death, decomposition, and re-release. Mycorrhizal fungi are critical intermediaries — their hyphae access soil volumes far beyond root reach and are the primary agents of phosphorus acquisition for most terrestrial plants (Smith & Read 2008). The biological cycle can turn over the available phosphorus pool many times per year, but it cannot create new phosphorus.

### Why phosphorus is the ultimate limiting nutrient

On long timescales (centuries to millennia), phosphorus availability controls ecosystem productivity because:

1. Nitrogen can be fixed from the atmosphere — phosphorus cannot.
2. Rock weathering rates are slow and decline as soils age.
3. Phosphorus binds strongly to iron and aluminum oxides in acidic soils and to calcium in alkaline soils, making much of the total soil phosphorus biologically unavailable (occluded phosphorus).

Walker and Syers (1976) described the long-term trajectory: young soils have abundant mineral phosphorus but low biological activity. As ecosystems develop, phosphorus is progressively transferred from mineral to organic forms, with increasing amounts locked in occluded pools. Old, highly weathered soils (e.g., tropical Oxisols, Australian laterites) are chronically phosphorus-poor, and their ecosystems show clear phosphorus limitation (Vitousek et al. 2010).

## Stoichiometric constraints

Ecological stoichiometry (Sterner & Elser 2002) treats organisms as chemical compounds with characteristic elemental ratios, and asks what happens when the ratios of consumers and their food do not match.

### The core principle

Organisms maintain relatively constrained body C:N:P ratios (homeostasis), but their food sources vary widely in elemental composition. This mismatch drives fundamental ecological dynamics:

- **Herbivores** consume plant tissue with C:N ratios of 20-80:1, but maintain body C:N ratios of ~5-7:1. They must process large volumes of carbon-rich food to extract sufficient nitrogen, excreting carbon-rich waste. This excess carbon is respired or egested, and the waste products are nitrogen-poor — affecting downstream decomposition rates.
- **Decomposers** face the same constraint. Fungi (C:N ~11:1) and bacteria (C:N ~5:1) decomposing high-C:N substrates must immobilize nitrogen from the environment to maintain their stoichiometry. When substrate C:N exceeds roughly 25:1, decomposers become net nitrogen sinks rather than sources (Manzoni et al. 2008), effectively competing with plants for nitrogen.
- **Predators** consume food that is stoichiometrically similar to themselves (animal tissue eating animal tissue), so consumer-resource mismatch is smallest at higher trophic levels. This is one reason energy transfer efficiency increases with trophic level.

### Redfield ratio

In marine systems, Redfield (1958) discovered that the average C:N:P ratio of ocean plankton biomass (~106:16:1) matches the ratio of dissolved C:N:P in deep ocean water. This is not coincidence — it reflects a long-term feedback where biological uptake and remineralization have driven ocean chemistry toward the ratio demanded by the dominant organisms. The Redfield ratio provides a benchmark for stoichiometric analysis across aquatic ecosystems. Deviations from it indicate which nutrient is limiting.

### Stoichiometry and nutrient recycling

The consumer-resource stoichiometric mismatch has a system-level consequence: it determines the nutrient ratio of recycled material. Herbivores consuming nitrogen-poor plants preferentially retain nitrogen and release phosphorus (or vice versa), altering the N:P ratio of available nutrients and potentially shifting which nutrient limits primary production. Elser et al. (2000) showed that consumer-driven nutrient recycling can qualitatively change nutrient limitation regimes in aquatic systems — the identity of the dominant herbivore determines which nutrient limits algal growth.

## Feedback loops

The nutrient cycles described above do not operate independently. They are coupled through organisms and soil chemistry, producing feedback loops that can stabilize, destabilize, or lock ecosystems into particular states.

### Decomposition-production coupling (balancing)

Production creates organic matter. Decomposition mineralizes it back into inorganic nutrients that support production. This is the fundamental balancing loop of nutrient cycling: more production means more organic matter input to the decomposer pool, which (with a lag set by decomposition rate) means more nutrient release, supporting more production. The lag introduces dynamics — if decomposition is slow relative to production, nutrients accumulate in the organic pool and production is nutrient-limited. If decomposition is fast, nutrients cycle rapidly and production is limited by something else (light, water, CO2).

### Nutrient limitation on production (balancing)

Production draws down available nutrient pools. As the pool shrinks, production slows, reducing the draw-down rate. This is a classic stock-depletion negative feedback. It operates independently for each nutrient — nitrogen and phosphorus can impose separate, simultaneous limitations (Liebig's law of the minimum). The nutrient that runs out first is the proximate limiting factor, but which nutrient this is depends on the relative input rates, loss rates, and biological demand ratios.

### Eutrophication (reinforcing)

When nutrient inputs increase (naturally or anthropogenically), primary production accelerates. In aquatic systems, the extra biomass eventually dies and sinks, where its decomposition consumes dissolved oxygen. The resulting hypoxia kills aerobic organisms, and their decomposition adds further oxygen demand — a positive feedback. Simultaneously, anaerobic conditions in sediments promote phosphorus release from iron-bound forms (the "internal loading" feedback), which further stimulates algal production (Carpenter, Ludwig & Brock 1999). Eutrophication thus involves at least two reinforcing loops that can make the eutrophic state self-sustaining even after external nutrient inputs are reduced.

### Oligotrophic traps (reinforcing)

The inverse of eutrophication: nutrient-poor ecosystems can maintain themselves through reinforcing feedbacks. Low nutrient availability selects for plants with long-lived, well-defended, nutrient-poor leaves. This recalcitrant litter decomposes slowly, keeping nutrients locked in organic forms. Slow decomposition means low nutrient release, maintaining low availability. Fire can reinforce the trap by volatilizing nitrogen. Hobbie (1992) and Wardle et al. (2004) documented this feedback in boreal forests and heathlands — the vegetation actively maintains the nutrient poverty that selects for it.

### Nitrogen-phosphorus interaction (cross-cycle coupling)

Nitrogen fixation requires phosphorus (for ATP) and molybdenum. Where phosphorus is scarce, nitrogen fixation is constrained, and the system remains nitrogen-limited even if fixation could theoretically relieve the limitation. Conversely, where nitrogen is abundant, phosphorus acquisition by mycorrhizal fungi is stimulated (plants allocate more carbon to fungal partners), which can increase phosphorus cycling rates. The two nutrient cycles are thus coupled through biological demand, and the behavior of one depends on the state of the other (Vitousek et al. 2010).

## ABM and computational literature

### Compartment models

The dominant paradigm for computational nutrient cycling is the compartment (box-and-arrow) model, where ecosystem stocks are represented as pools and flows between them are governed by differential equations. Major examples:

- **CENTURY** (Parton et al. 1987): decomposes soil organic matter into active, slow, and passive pools with characteristic turnover times. Widely used for carbon and nitrogen cycling in grasslands and agricultural soils. Extended versions (DayCent) operate at daily timesteps.
- **BIOME-BGC** (Running & Hunt 1993): a process-based model of carbon, nitrogen, and water cycling in terrestrial ecosystems. Operates at daily timestep with explicit leaf-level photosynthesis, stomatal conductance, and soil decomposition. Used extensively for climate-carbon feedback studies.
- **RothC** (Coleman & Jenkinson 1996): a simpler soil carbon turnover model partitioning organic matter into five compartments. Designed for agricultural applications.

These models capture system-level nutrient dynamics effectively but treat organism populations as aggregated stocks rather than individual agents. They cannot represent spatial heterogeneity arising from individual behavior, local interactions, or network topology.

### Agent-based approaches

Agent-based models of nutrient cycling are less common than compartment models but are growing in use:

- **Individual-based forest models** (SORTIE, FORMIND, iLand): model individual trees competing for light and nutrients, with nutrient cycling emerging from individual-level uptake, litterfall, and decomposition. These capture spatial heterogeneity in nutrient availability and the local feedbacks between trees and soil (Seidl et al. 2012).
- **Microbial ABMs**: some models represent individual microbial cells or colonies decomposing substrate in spatially explicit soil environments (Allison 2012, Kaiser et al. 2015). These reveal how microbial spatial structure — colonies, hotspots, biofilms — affects bulk decomposition rates in ways that well-mixed compartment models cannot capture.
- **Soil food web models**: DeAngelis (1992) and subsequent work model nutrient cycling through networks of interacting functional groups (bacteria, fungi, protozoa, nematodes, microarthropods) with explicit feeding relationships. Individual-based variants represent spatial patchiness in soil.

### What ABMs add

Agent-based approaches to nutrient cycling capture dynamics that compartment models miss:

1. **Spatial heterogeneity.** Nutrient availability is patchy at fine scales — around individual root zones, decaying logs, animal carcasses. Local feedbacks between organisms and their immediate nutrient environment can produce landscape-level patterns (nutrient hotspots, depletion zones) that matter for community dynamics.
2. **Individual variation.** Different individual plants of the same species may have different mycorrhizal partners, different litter chemistry, or different root architectures, leading to local variation in nutrient cycling rates.
3. **Network effects.** Mycorrhizal networks create non-local nutrient flows — resources acquired in one location can be transported to and released in another. This network topology cannot be represented in compartment models without explicit spatial structure.
4. **Emergent stoichiometry.** If individual agents have variable stoichiometry (rather than fixed pool ratios), the aggregate nutrient ratios of waste, recycled material, and available nutrients emerge from individual-level consumption decisions rather than being parameterized.

## Multi-currency dynamics

Real ecosystems operate on multiple currencies simultaneously — carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, water — and much of their complexity arises from the interactions between these currencies rather than from any single one.

### What energy alone captures

Thermodynamic structure is fundamentally an energy phenomenon. Energy enters via primary production, flows through trophic levels with transfer losses at each step, and is dissipated by metabolism. The resulting trophic pyramid, the ~10% transfer efficiency between levels (Lindeman 1942), and the exponential decay of available energy up the food chain are all energy-flow properties. Consumer-resource dynamics — predator-prey oscillations, competitive exclusion, density-dependent regulation — likewise depend on the rate of energy acquisition relative to metabolic cost, not on the identity of the nutrient. The structural necessity of decomposition (dead organic matter must be processed for the system to persist) is also an energy-flow phenomenon.

### What requires multiple currencies

**Differential limitation.** Producers may be nitrogen-limited while decomposers are carbon-limited (or vice versa). This differential limitation creates the immobilization-mineralization dynamic: decomposers competing with plants for nitrogen when substrate C:N is high. With a single currency, there is only one limiting factor, and this cross-trophic competition for different nutrients cannot occur.

**Stoichiometric mismatch.** The central insight of Sterner & Elser (2002) — that consumer-resource elemental mismatches drive consumption rates, waste composition, and nutrient recycling — requires at least two currencies. A herbivore eating a nitrogen-poor plant and excreting carbon-rich waste is a two-currency phenomenon.

**Nutrient-specific feedback loops.** Eutrophication is driven by excess phosphorus (or nitrogen) specifically — not by excess energy in general. Oligotrophic traps are maintained by nitrogen volatilization in fire and phosphorus occlusion in soil. The nitrogen fixation constraint (limited by phosphorus and molybdenum) creates a cross-cycle coupling. None of these dynamics emerge from energy flow alone.

**Alternative stable states driven by nutrient ratios.** Shifts between nitrogen-limited and phosphorus-limited states, or between oligotrophic and eutrophic states, are driven by the ratio of multiple nutrients, not by the total amount of any single currency. These regime shifts require at least two currencies.

**Litter quality effects.** Decomposition rate depends on substrate quality — C:N ratio, lignin content, phosphorus concentration. A single-currency dead-matter pool decomposes at a rate determined only by its quantity and the decomposer population, not by who produced it. When litter from different producers decomposes at different rates, the litter-soil feedback shapes succession and alternative stable states.

### The currency gradient

Ecological dynamics can be characterized by how many currencies are needed to explain them:

1. **Energy only.** Trophic structure and basic consumer-resource dynamics. Misses nutrient-specific limitation, stoichiometric mismatch, and quality-dependent decomposition.
2. **Energy + one nutrient.** Adds limitation dynamics and producer-decomposer competition. A single nutrient (acting as a proxy for nitrogen or phosphorus) enables differential limitation and litter quality effects. This is the minimum for stoichiometric mismatch.
3. **Carbon + nitrogen + phosphorus.** Full stoichiometric dynamics including N:P ratio shifts, cross-cycle coupling, and nutrient-specific feedback loops.
4. **Three currencies + water.** Water acts as both a nutrient and a transport medium. Adds drought dynamics, leaching, and hydrological nutrient transport.

The most common approach in individual-based ecology models is option 2 — a single "quality" axis for resources alongside an energy axis — because it captures the qualitative dynamics of nutrient limitation and stoichiometric mismatch without requiring full biogeochemical parameterization (Manzoni & Porporato 2009).
