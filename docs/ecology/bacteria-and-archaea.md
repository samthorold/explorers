# Bacteria and Archaea

Bacteria and archaea are the metabolic backbone of every ecosystem on Earth. From a systems ecology perspective, they are best understood not as individual organisms but as the primary mediators of biogeochemical flows — the agents that close nutrient loops, unlock energy from inorganic substrates, and regulate the chemical boundary conditions within which all other life operates. Without them, ecosystems are not merely degraded; they cease to function as thermodynamic systems.

## Systemic role

In stock-and-flow terms, microbes occupy three critical positions simultaneously:

**Decomposers and recyclers.** Dead organic matter is a stock that accumulates unless something mineralises it back into inorganic nutrients. Bacteria and fungi are the only agents that perform this conversion at ecosystem scale. Bacterial decomposition dominates in aquatic systems and in soils with near-neutral to alkaline pH; fungal decomposition dominates in acidic, lignin-rich terrestrial systems (Wardle et al. 2004). The systemic distinction matters: bacterial decomposition is fast and nutrient-leaky (rapid mineralisation, high nutrient turnover), while fungal decomposition is slow and nutrient-retentive (immobilisation into hyphal networks). The bacterial:fungal decomposition ratio is itself a control variable on ecosystem nutrient dynamics (Six et al. 2006).

**Nutrient transformers.** Many essential nutrients exist in forms that are biologically unavailable until microbes convert them. Atmospheric N2 is inert until nitrogen-fixing bacteria (free-living or symbiotic) reduce it to ammonium. Ammonium is unavailable to most plants until nitrifying bacteria and archaea oxidise it to nitrate. Phosphorus locked in mineral form is solubilised by microbial organic acid excretion. Sulphur cycling depends almost entirely on bacterial oxidation and reduction. Microbes are not merely participants in nutrient cycles — they are the rate-limiting agents of the transformations that define those cycles.

**Primary producers (chemoautotrophs and cyanobacteria).** In environments without light — hydrothermal vents, deep subsurface, anoxic sediments — chemoautotrophic bacteria and archaea are the sole entry point for energy into the food web, fixing inorganic carbon using chemical energy from reduced compounds (H2S, NH3, Fe2+, H2). Even in sunlit ecosystems, cyanobacteria contribute substantially to primary production (see below).

**The microbial loop.** In aquatic systems, Azam et al. (1983) described the "microbial loop" — a pathway where dissolved organic carbon (DOC) leaked by phytoplankton is taken up by heterotrophic bacteria, which are then consumed by protists, which are consumed by zooplankton, returning energy to the classical food chain. This loop recovers energy that would otherwise be lost from the system as dissolved organic matter. It can process 30-50% of total primary production in marine systems (Fenchel 2008). From a flows perspective, the microbial loop is a scavenging subsystem that reduces leakage from the main production-consumption pathway.

## Energy and nutrient flows

### Carbon

Microbes mediate both the fixation and release of carbon. Autotrophic microbes (cyanobacteria, chemoautotrophs) fix CO2 into organic carbon. Heterotrophic microbes respire organic carbon back to CO2. The balance between these two flows — the microbial carbon balance — determines whether a given environment is a net carbon sink or source. In soils, microbial respiration is the dominant pathway for carbon loss, releasing approximately 60 Pg C yr-1 globally (Bond-Lamberty & Thomson 2010). Methanogenic archaea in anaerobic environments produce methane (CH4), a potent greenhouse gas; methanotrophic bacteria oxidise it. This methanogenesis-methanotrophy pair is a microbial flow controller on a globally significant carbon stock.

### Nitrogen

The nitrogen cycle is almost entirely microbially mediated:

- **Fixation** (N2 -> NH4+): Free-living diazotrophs (Azotobacter, cyanobacteria) and symbiotic rhizobia convert atmospheric N2 to biologically available ammonium. This is the primary natural input of new nitrogen to terrestrial ecosystems, estimated at 100-290 Tg N yr-1 globally (Vitousek et al. 2013).
- **Nitrification** (NH4+ -> NO2- -> NO3-): Ammonia-oxidising bacteria (Nitrosomonas) and archaea (Nitrosopumilus), followed by nitrite-oxidising bacteria (Nitrobacter), convert ammonium to nitrate. This transformation determines the dominant form of plant-available nitrogen.
- **Denitrification** (NO3- -> N2O -> N2): Facultative anaerobes (Pseudomonas, Paracoccus) reduce nitrate back to atmospheric nitrogen under low-oxygen conditions. This is the primary nitrogen exit from ecosystems. The intermediate product N2O is a greenhouse gas 300x more potent than CO2 per molecule.
- **Ammonification**: Decomposer microbes break down organic N in dead matter back to ammonium, completing the internal recycling loop.

The systemic consequence: microbes control both the input (fixation) and output (denitrification) of nitrogen in ecosystems, plus the internal conversions between nitrogen forms. No other taxon has leverage over an entire elemental cycle at this level.

### Phosphorus and sulphur

Phosphorus cycling is slower and less microbially dominated than nitrogen, but microbes are still critical. Phosphate-solubilising bacteria release bound phosphorus from mineral surfaces through organic acid production. Mycorrhizal fungi (which partner with bacteria in the rhizosphere) extend the effective root surface area for phosphorus uptake by orders of magnitude.

Sulphur cycling in anaerobic environments is almost entirely microbial. Sulphate-reducing bacteria (Desulfovibrio) use sulphate as a terminal electron acceptor, producing H2S. Sulphur-oxidising bacteria (Thiobacillus, Beggiatoa) reverse this. These paired flows create the redox gradients that structure anaerobic sediment communities.

## Feedback loops

Microbes participate in several feedback loops that stabilise or destabilise ecosystems:

**Decomposition-production positive feedback.** Decomposer microbes mineralise dead organic matter into inorganic nutrients, which fuel primary production, which generates more organic matter for decomposition. This is the fundamental nutrient recycling loop. Its speed is controlled by microbial activity, which is in turn controlled by temperature, moisture, and substrate quality (Chapin et al. 2011). Disrupting the decomposer stock (e.g., through sterilisation or extreme pH shift) breaks this loop, causing nutrient lock-up in dead organic matter and eventual production collapse.

**Nitrogen fixation negative feedback.** Nitrogen fixation is energetically expensive (16 ATP per N2 molecule). Nitrogen-fixing microbes are competitively disadvantaged when soil nitrogen is abundant, so fixation declines as nitrogen accumulates — a self-regulating negative feedback on the nitrogen stock. When nitrogen is depleted (by plant uptake, leaching, or denitrification), fixation increases again. This stabilises ecosystem nitrogen within a range (Menge et al. 2012).

**Oxygen-redox state feedback.** Cyanobacterial photosynthesis produces O2, which shifts the redox state of the environment toward oxidising conditions. This favours aerobic heterotrophs and nitrifiers but inhibits anaerobic processes like denitrification, methanogenesis, and sulphate reduction. The balance between O2 production and consumption by heterotrophic microbes determines whether an environment is aerobic or anaerobic — a bistable switch with enormous consequences for which chemical pathways are active (Canfield et al. 2005). At geological timescales, the Great Oxidation Event (~2.4 Ga) was cyanobacteria tipping this switch globally.

**Soil aggregate feedback.** Bacterial biofilms and fungal hyphae physically stabilise soil aggregates, which improves water retention and aeration, which improves conditions for microbial growth — a positive feedback that builds soil structure over time (Tisdall & Oades 1982, Six et al. 2004). Disturbance (tillage, erosion) breaks this loop.

**pH feedback.** Nitrification produces H+ ions, acidifying the soil. Acidification shifts microbial community composition toward fungi and away from bacteria, reducing nitrification rate — a negative feedback. Conversely, denitrification and ammonification consume H+, raising pH. The microbial community thus buffers soil pH within ranges, though external forcing (acid rain, liming) can overwhelm the buffer capacity (Rousk et al. 2009).

## Key strategies

### Chemoautotrophy

Chemoautotrophic microbes derive energy from the oxidation of inorganic compounds rather than light. From a flows perspective, they represent an alternative energy input pathway — converting geochemical energy into biological energy. Key substrates and their associated energy yields:

- Hydrogen oxidation (H2 + O2): Used by Hydrogenophaga and deep-subsurface lithotrophs. Supports the deep biosphere, estimated to contain 2-19% of Earth's total biomass (Bar-On et al. 2018).
- Sulphide oxidation (H2S + O2): Drives hydrothermal vent ecosystems. Vent-associated chemoautotrophs sustain food webs completely independent of solar energy (Karl et al. 1980).
- Ammonia oxidation (NH3 + O2): The first step of nitrification, performed by ammonia-oxidising archaea (Thaumarchaeota) and bacteria. These organisms couple their energy metabolism directly to a key nutrient transformation.
- Iron oxidation (Fe2+ -> Fe3+): Common in acidic, iron-rich environments. Iron-oxidising bacteria (Acidithiobacillus) are major agents of acid mine drainage.

The systemic implication: chemoautotrophy decouples biological production from light. Any environment with a sustained redox gradient — chemical disequilibrium — can support a microbial ecosystem. This is why the deep subsurface, ocean floor, and extraterrestrial environments (e.g., Europa's hypothesised subsurface ocean) are considered plausible habitats.

### Decomposition

Bacterial decomposition and fungal decomposition are systemically distinct strategies:

| Property | Bacterial decomposition | Fungal decomposition |
|---|---|---|
| Preferred substrate | Labile C (sugars, amino acids) | Recalcitrant C (lignin, cellulose) |
| Turnover rate | Fast (hours to days) | Slow (weeks to months) |
| C:N ratio preference | Low C:N (N-rich material) | High C:N (N-poor material) |
| Nutrient dynamics | Net mineralisation (nutrients released) | Net immobilisation (nutrients retained in biomass) |
| Spatial scale | Local, within water films | Extended, via hyphal networks |
| pH preference | Neutral to alkaline | Acidic |

(Based on Wardle et al. 2004; de Boer et al. 2005)

In ecosystem models, the bacterial:fungal ratio functions as a switch between "fast" and "slow" nutrient cycling regimes. Bacterial-dominated systems (grasslands, agricultural soils) cycle nutrients rapidly with high leakage. Fungal-dominated systems (forests, peatlands) cycle nutrients slowly with high retention. Shifts between these regimes can be triggered by disturbance, pH change, or litter quality changes.

### Nitrogen fixation

From a flows perspective, nitrogen fixation is the primary input valve for the ecosystem nitrogen budget. The key systemic feature is its conditionality: fixation is suppressed when inorganic nitrogen is already available (energetic cost makes fixers uncompetitive) and activated when nitrogen is scarce. This makes nitrogen fixation a demand-driven input rather than a constant flux. Symbiotic fixation (rhizobia in legume root nodules) couples the nitrogen input directly to plant demand, with the plant paying the energy cost (photosynthate) in exchange for nitrogen — a mutualistic exchange of energy for a limiting nutrient.

### Parasitism and pathogenesis

Bacterial pathogens represent a top-down flow: they reduce host fitness, divert host energy to their own reproduction, and — when lethal — convert host biomass to dead organic matter for the decomposition pathway. At the population level, pathogens function as density-dependent regulators. Dense host populations are more susceptible to transmission, so pathogen outbreaks suppress host density — a negative feedback on host population growth that can prevent competitive exclusion and maintain biodiversity (the Janzen-Connell hypothesis for plant pathogens; Bagchi et al. 2014). Bacterial pathogens are particularly important in soil, where soil-borne pathogens create "negative plant-soil feedback" — plants perform worse when grown in soil conditioned by their own species, promoting species coexistence.

### Biofilm formation

Biofilms are spatially structured microbial communities encased in extracellular polymeric substances (EPS). From a systems perspective, biofilm formation is a spatial aggregation strategy that modifies local environmental conditions. Within a biofilm, microbes create steep chemical gradients (O2, pH, nutrients) across distances of micrometres, enabling metabolically diverse consortia to coexist. The outer layer may be aerobic while the interior is anaerobic, allowing coupled nitrification-denitrification within a single structure (Stewart & Franklin 2008). Biofilms on surfaces also resist physical removal (flow, grazing), increasing microbial persistence. In streams, biofilms are the dominant site of nutrient transformation (Battin et al. 2016).

## Cyanobacteria as producers

Cyanobacteria occupy a unique systemic position: they are prokaryotes (bacteria) that perform oxygenic photosynthesis. They are the evolutionary ancestors of chloroplasts and were the original source of atmospheric oxygen.

**Contribution to primary production.** Marine cyanobacteria (Prochlorococcus, Synechococcus) are the most abundant photosynthetic organisms on Earth. Prochlorococcus alone accounts for an estimated 8-15% of global ocean net primary production (Flombaum et al. 2013). In freshwater systems, cyanobacteria dominate under eutrophic, warm, stratified conditions — their buoyancy regulation via gas vesicles gives them a competitive advantage over sinking phytoplankton (Paerl & Huisman 2009).

**Nitrogen fixation + photosynthesis.** Some cyanobacteria (Anabaena, Trichodesmium) fix nitrogen while also photosynthesising. This combination makes them independent of both external energy and nitrogen inputs — they are self-sufficient primary producers in the fullest sense. In oligotrophic ocean gyres, Trichodesmium is a major nitrogen source, coupling atmospheric N2 to marine production (Capone et al. 2005). The systemic consequence is that these organisms can colonise nutrient-depleted environments and bootstrap productivity from essentially nothing: light + CO2 + N2 + water.

**Blooms as system state transitions.** Under eutrophication (excess phosphorus or nitrogen loading), cyanobacterial blooms represent a regime shift in aquatic systems. The bloom state is maintained by positive feedback: dense cyanobacteria shade out competitors, reduce mixing by absorbing heat at the surface, and produce toxins that deter grazing (Scheffer et al. 2001). Bloom collapse releases massive organic carbon pulses that fuel bacterial decomposition, which draws down dissolved oxygen, creating hypoxic dead zones — a cascading failure where microbial production overwhelms the system's respiratory capacity.

**Biological soil crusts.** In arid terrestrial systems, cyanobacteria (with lichens, mosses, and fungi) form biological soil crusts that fix both carbon and nitrogen, stabilise soil surfaces against erosion, and modulate water infiltration. These crusts are the primary production base in desert ecosystems (Belnap & Lange 2003).

## ABM and computational literature

Agent-based and individual-based models (ABMs/IBMs) of microbial ecology have grown substantially since the 2000s, driven by interest in biofilm dynamics, soil microbiome function, and marine microbial ecology.

### Biofilm models

**iDynoMiCS** (individual-based Dynamics of Microbial Communities Simulator) is the most widely used ABM framework for bacterial biofilms. It models individual bacterial cells as spherical agents that grow, divide, and interact in a 2D or 3D spatial domain with diffusion-reaction equations for substrates. It has been used to study nitrification-denitrification coupling in biofilms, interspecies competition, and spatial structure emergence (Lardon et al. 2011). An updated version, **iDynoMiCS 2.0**, adds flexible agent types and improved computational performance (Naylor et al. 2017).

**BacSim** (Kreft et al. 1998) was an early individual-based model of bacterial growth in spatially structured environments. It demonstrated that spatial structure alone — without any prescribed cooperation mechanism — could generate stable coexistence of competing bacterial strains.

**NUFEB** (Newcastle University Frontiers in Engineering Biology) is a more recent biofilm ABM built on the LAMMPS molecular dynamics engine, allowing large-scale simulations of millions of individual cells with mechanical interactions, nutrient diffusion, and metabolic activity (Li et al. 2019).

### Soil and rhizosphere models

**IndiMeSH** (Individual-based Model of the Soil Habitat) models soil bacteria as individual agents in a 3D pore network, with water films, nutrient diffusion, and heterogeneous soil structure. It demonstrated that soil spatial heterogeneity is a primary driver of microbial community diversity — even metabolically identical agents differentiate into distinct local populations due to spatial isolation (Kalman et al. 2018).

Soil carbon decomposition models have increasingly incorporated microbial physiology. The **MIMICS** (Microbial-Mineral Carbon Stabilization) model, while not strictly agent-based, represents microbial functional groups (copiotrophic vs. oligotrophic bacteria, roughly paralleling the bacterial vs. fungal decomposition distinction) as explicit pools with distinct kinetics (Wieder et al. 2015). This approach outperformed traditional first-order decay models in predicting soil carbon responses to warming.

Kaiser et al. (2014) developed an ABM of soil organic matter decomposition that modelled individual microbial agents producing extracellular enzymes. The model showed that microbial community dynamics — not just substrate availability — were a primary control on decomposition rates, supporting the "microbial efficiency-matrix stabilisation" (MEMS) framework.

### Marine microbial loop models

**Follows et al. (2007)** implemented an influential model of marine phytoplankton communities (including cyanobacteria such as Prochlorococcus and Synechococcus) with trait-based diversity. While not strictly agent-based (it used Eulerian grid-based population dynamics), it demonstrated that observed global biogeography of phytoplankton functional types could emerge from simple physiological trade-offs — a result relevant to ABM approaches.

**Hellweger & Bucci (2009)** developed an individual-based model of Synechococcus populations that tracked individual cell physiology (nutrient quotas, growth rate, cell division) and demonstrated that individual-level variability produced population dynamics not captured by mean-field models.

Microbial loop models (e.g., Thingstad et al. 2007) have explored how viral lysis of bacteria ("kill the winner" dynamics) maintains bacterial diversity by preventing dominance — a finding with direct implications for ABM design: predation pressure and population control via phage/virus agents can be a powerful diversity-maintenance mechanism.

### General frameworks and reviews

Hellweger et al. (2016) provide a comprehensive review of agent-based modelling in microbial ecology, covering model design patterns, computational challenges, and validation approaches. They identify key advantages of ABMs over population-level models: (1) emergent spatial structure, (2) individual heterogeneity effects, (3) history-dependent behaviour (e.g., lag phases), and (4) stochastic effects at low population densities.

Widder et al. (2016) reviewed computational approaches to modelling microbial communities in environmental contexts, comparing ABMs with differential equation models, metabolic flux models, and statistical approaches. They note that ABMs excel at capturing spatial interactions and community assembly but face computational limits when scaling to realistic population sizes (10^8 - 10^12 cells per gram of soil).

## System without microbes

Removing bacteria and archaea from an ecosystem is not a perturbation — it is a termination of most biogeochemical cycling.

**Immediate effects (days to weeks):**
- Decomposition of dead organic matter effectively ceases. Carcasses, leaf litter, and dissolved organic matter accumulate. The detrital stock grows without limit.
- Nitrogen fixation stops. No new nitrogen enters the biological system. Existing inorganic nitrogen is consumed by plants and not replenished.
- Nitrification stops. Ammonium accumulates; nitrate is depleted. Plants adapted to nitrate uptake (most temperate species) lose their nitrogen source.
- Ruminant and termite digestion fails. Herbivores dependent on microbial gut symbionts for cellulose breakdown starve despite abundant food.

**Medium-term effects (weeks to months):**
- Plant-available nitrogen and phosphorus decline toward zero as existing stocks are consumed and not recycled. Primary production collapses.
- Aquatic systems lose 30-50% of their energy cycling capacity (the microbial loop). Dissolved organic carbon accumulates.
- Anoxic environments lose their energy base entirely (chemoautotrophic production ceases). Hydrothermal vent communities collapse completely.

**Long-term effects (months to years):**
- Terrestrial ecosystems converge on a state of accumulated dead organic matter with declining living biomass — essentially a world of thickening litter with starving plants. The system runs out of accessible nutrients even though total elemental stocks (locked in dead matter) may be high.
- Atmospheric composition shifts. CO2 drawdown by photosynthesis declines as production collapses. Methane production ceases, but so does methane oxidation. N2O production ceases. The net atmospheric effect is complex but the cessation of biological nutrient cycling is the dominant signal.
- The ocean loses its biological carbon pump. Dead organic matter in surface waters is not remineralised, so the vertical flux of nutrients from the surface to the deep (and back via upwelling) is disrupted.

In systems terms: microbes are not a trophic level that can be compensated for by other organisms. They are the infrastructure of elemental cycling. Removing them is analogous to removing the circulatory system from an animal — the organs (plants, animals, fungi) may be intact, but the flows that sustain them stop.
