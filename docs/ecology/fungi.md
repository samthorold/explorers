# Fungi

Fungi occupy a unique position in ecosystem architecture: they are the primary agents responsible for closing nutrient loops that would otherwise break open. Where producers fix energy and consumers transfer it up trophic levels, fungi operate across the system's seams — decomposing locked organic matter back into bioavailable nutrients, forming resource-sharing infrastructure between producers, and regulating population dynamics through parasitism. From a systems dynamics perspective, fungi are less a trophic level and more a set of coupling mechanisms that connect stocks and regulate flows throughout the system.

## Systemic role

In systems ecology terms, fungi serve three distinct systemic functions:

1. **Nutrient liberators (saprotrophic).** Dead organic matter represents a stock of locked energy and nutrients — carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, sulfur. Without enzymatic decomposition, this stock grows monotonically. Saprotrophic fungi are the primary agents capable of breaking down recalcitrant compounds like lignin and cellulose, converting locked stocks into bioavailable flows. They are the rate-limiting step in the detrital pathway of energy flow (Moore et al. 2004).

2. **Resource-redistribution infrastructure (mycorrhizal).** Mycorrhizal fungi form networks connecting the root systems of multiple plants, creating a transport layer for carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, and water. This transforms the nutrient landscape from a collection of isolated agents competing for local soil resources into a partially connected system where resources can flow between producers based on supply and demand gradients. The network topology exhibits scale-free and small-world properties (Simard 2018), meaning a few highly-connected hub nodes (large established trees) disproportionately influence system-wide resource distribution.

3. **Population regulators (parasitic).** Fungal pathogens create density-dependent mortality, acting as a negative feedback on host populations. This prevents any single species from monopolising resources indefinitely and maintains diversity through mechanisms analogous to Janzen-Connell effects — high-density populations of a single species become increasingly vulnerable to specialist pathogens.

These three roles map onto a single systemic principle: fungi prevent the accumulation of any stock to the point where it destabilises the system — whether that stock is dead organic matter, spatially concentrated nutrients, or a monoculture population.

## Energy and nutrient flows

Fungi participate in two of the major energy pathways identified in ecosystem ecology:

**The detrital pathway.** Lindeman (1942) established that ecosystems have two primary energy channels: the grazer pathway (energy flows from living producers to consumers) and the detrital pathway (energy flows from dead organic matter through decomposers). In most terrestrial ecosystems, the detrital pathway processes the majority of net primary production — often 80-90% of plant biomass enters the dead organic matter pool rather than being consumed live.

Fungi are the dominant decomposers of plant-derived organic matter because they produce extracellular enzymes (lignin peroxidases, manganese peroxidases, laccases) capable of breaking down lignin, the structural polymer that makes up 15-40% of plant dry mass. Bacteria cannot efficiently decompose lignin. This gives fungi a near-monopoly on the rate-limiting step of terrestrial decomposition.

Key flow parameters:
- **Substrate quality controls decomposition rate.** The lignin:nitrogen ratio of organic matter is the best single predictor of decomposition rate. High lignin:N material (wood) decomposes slowly; low lignin:N material (leaf litter) decomposes quickly.
- **Carbon use efficiency.** Fungi convert roughly 40-55% of consumed carbon into biomass, with the remainder respired as CO2. This is substantially higher than bacterial carbon use efficiency (~20-30%), meaning fungi sequester more carbon per unit consumed into the microbial biomass stock.
- **Nutrient stoichiometry.** Fungal biomass has an average C:N ratio of ~11:1 (Brabcova et al. 2024), but wood-decay fungi can reach C:N ratios above 40:1. Fungi must acquire nitrogen from their substrate or soil environment, creating a coupling between decomposition rate and nitrogen availability.

**The mycorrhizal pathway.** Mycorrhizal fungi create a bidirectional flow: they receive 10-30% of a host plant's photosynthetically fixed carbon, and in return supply the plant with soil-derived phosphorus, nitrogen, and water. This is not charity — it is a market-like exchange governed by local supply-demand ratios (Kiers et al. 2011, "Reciprocal rewards stabilize cooperation in the mycorrhizal symbiosis"). Plants allocate more carbon to fungal partners that deliver more phosphorus, and fungi allocate more phosphorus to plant partners that deliver more carbon.

At the system level, this creates an energy flow that bypasses the detrital pathway entirely: carbon fixed by producers flows directly into the soil fungal network, fueling nutrient acquisition that feeds back into producer growth.

## Feedback loops

Fungi participate in several reinforcing and balancing feedback loops:

**Reinforcing: Decomposition-growth loop.**
Dead organic matter --> fungal decomposition --> nutrient release to soil --> producer uptake and growth --> more organic matter production --> more dead organic matter. This is the fundamental nutrient cycling loop of terrestrial ecosystems. Fungi set the speed of this loop through their decomposition rate.

**Reinforcing: Mycorrhizal mutualism loop.**
Healthy producers --> more carbon allocation to mycorrhizal fungi --> larger fungal network --> more nutrient acquisition --> healthier producers. This positive feedback amplifies producer productivity in nutrient-poor environments and is a major driver of forest establishment and succession.

**Balancing: Nutrient limitation on decomposition.**
Rapid decomposition --> nutrient release --> producer growth --> increased lignin production (as plants grow larger and woodier) --> slower decomposition of high-lignin material. As ecosystems mature, the average substrate quality declines, slowing the decomposition loop. This is a key mechanism behind carbon accumulation in old-growth forests.

**Balancing: Parasitic density-dependence.**
High host density --> increased pathogen transmission --> host mortality --> reduced host density --> reduced pathogen transmission. Fungal pathogens create classic boom-bust dynamics (Burdon & Laine 2019) that prevent competitive dominance and maintain species diversity. This is a stabilising force at the community level.

**Balancing: Mycorrhizal resource redistribution.**
Shade/stress on some producers --> reduced photosynthesis --> those producers receive more nutrients via mycorrhizal network from hub trees --> partial recovery. The mycorrhizal network acts as a buffer against local resource shocks, damping oscillations in producer populations. The extent and significance of this redistribution is actively debated in the literature (Karst et al. 2023).

## Key strategies (saprotrophic, mycorrhizal, parasitic — from a flows perspective)

These are not taxonomic categories but functional strategies defined by their position in the system's flow architecture:

### Saprotrophic strategy

**Flow position:** Dead organic matter stock --> fungal biomass + bioavailable nutrient flow to soil.

Saprotrophic fungi are the system's recyclers. They consume a stock (dead organic matter) that no other functional group can efficiently process. Their decomposition rate determines how fast the locked nutrient stock turns over.

Key system-level distinctions among saprotrophs:
- **White-rot fungi** decompose both lignin and cellulose, leaving a bleached residue. They are the only organisms capable of fully mineralising lignin. Without them, woody material accumulates — the Carboniferous period (360-300 Mya) saw massive coal formation in part because lignin-degrading Agaricomycetes had not yet evolved their full enzymatic arsenal (Floudas et al. 2012, Science). Once white-rot fungi appeared (~290 Mya), the rate of coal formation dropped sharply, though the relative contribution of fungal evolution vs. climate and tectonics remains debated (Nelsen et al. 2016, PNAS).
- **Brown-rot fungi** decompose cellulose but leave lignin largely intact, producing a brown, crumbly residue that contributes to stable soil organic matter (humus). They create a flow from labile carbon to the atmosphere while routing recalcitrant carbon into long-term soil stocks.
- **Soft-rot fungi** decompose cellulose in high-moisture, low-oxygen environments where white and brown-rot fungi are less competitive — a niche-specific decomposition pathway.

### Mycorrhizal strategy

**Flow position:** Producer carbon stock --> fungal biomass; soil nutrient stock --> producer nutrient uptake (mediated by fungi).

Mycorrhizal fungi are the system's connective tissue. They create flows between stocks that would otherwise be uncoupled:

- **Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF)** associate with ~80% of land plant species. They primarily enhance phosphorus uptake. The network is relatively non-specific — many plant species connect through the same fungal network, creating broad resource-sharing potential.
- **Ectomycorrhizal fungi (ECM)** associate primarily with trees in temperate and boreal forests. They form dense hyphal mantles around root tips and can decompose soil organic matter to access organically bound nitrogen (Lindahl et al. 2007). This dual role — mutualist and decomposer — means ECM fungi create a direct coupling between the carbon-for-nutrients exchange and the decomposition loop.

From a network perspective, the mycorrhizal strategy transforms point-to-point resource competition into a network flow problem. The implications for system dynamics are significant: connected plant communities show reduced variance in individual fitness, greater resilience to local perturbations, and altered competitive dynamics (van der Heijden et al. 1998).

### Parasitic strategy

**Flow position:** Living host energy stock --> fungal biomass (at host's expense).

Parasitic fungi extract energy from living organisms, creating a direct drain on host fitness. System-level effects:

- **Density-dependent regulation.** Transmission increases with host density, creating negative feedback that caps population growth. This is functionally equivalent to a predator in the grazer pathway but operates within the producer trophic level.
- **Diversity maintenance.** Specialist pathogens disproportionately impact dominant species (the species with the highest local density), creating openings for competitors. This is a key mechanism behind the high diversity of tropical forests (Bagchi et al. 2014).
- **Boom-bust dynamics.** Host-pathogen systems frequently exhibit oscillatory dynamics — host populations grow until pathogen pressure builds, then crash, allowing recovery when pathogen populations decline due to reduced host availability.

## ABM and computational literature

Agent-based and individual-based models of fungal systems are relatively sparse compared to, say, predator-prey or vegetation dynamics models, but several lines of work are directly relevant:

### Hyphal growth and mycelial network models

**Falconer et al. (2005, 2007, 2012).** Ruth Falconer and colleagues developed individual-based models of fungal colony growth where individual hyphal tips are the agents. These models incorporate nutrient absorption, intramycelial transport, biomass recycling, and branching decisions. Key insight: colony-level behaviour (radial growth rate, density patterns, resource allocation) emerges from simple hyphal-tip rules interacting with local nutrient fields. Published in *Fungal Genetics and Biology* and *Proceedings of the Royal Society B*. The work connects to the Grimm/Railsback tradition of pattern-oriented modeling — colony-level patterns are used to validate individual-level rules.

**Boswell et al. (2007).** Developed a hybrid model combining a continuous nutrient field with discrete hyphal agents, showing how nutrient translocation within networks leads to non-trivial foraging patterns. The model demonstrates that fungal networks can exhibit adaptive resource allocation without centralised control — a key property for ABM implementations.

### Mycorrhizal network models

**Simard and colleagues.** While Simard's primary work is empirical, it has generated modeling work on mycorrhizal networks as graphs. The network topology (scale-free, small-world) has been characterised and modeled using network science approaches. The key modeling implication: a few hub nodes (mother trees) disproportionately control resource flows, and their removal causes cascading effects on seedling survival.

**Fellbaum et al. (2014) / Kiers et al. (2011).** These studies modeled the mycorrhizal symbiosis as a biological market, where resource exchange ratios between plants and fungi are governed by local supply-demand dynamics. This market framing translates directly into ABM architectures: agents (plants, fungi) exchange resources based on local prices, and system-level allocation patterns emerge.

### Decomposition and nutrient cycling models

**Moore et al. (2005) — Detritus, trophic dynamics, and biodiversity.** While not strictly an ABM, this work models the detrital food web as a system of interacting functional groups, showing how energy flow through the decomposer pathway (dominated by fungi and bacteria) interacts with the grazer pathway to determine community structure and stability. Provides a systems-dynamics framework that could be implemented as an ABM.

**Moore et al. (2015) — Decomposition by ectomycorrhizal fungi alters soil carbon storage.** A simulation model showing that when ECM fungi decompose soil organic matter (the "Gadgil effect"), it alters the balance between carbon inputs and outputs in forest soils. Demonstrates the importance of the dual decomposer-mutualist role of ECM fungi for system-level carbon dynamics.

**CENTURY and RothC models.** These are not ABMs but compartment models of soil carbon turnover that represent fungal vs. bacterial decomposition pathways with different turnover rates. They provide baseline parameters (decomposition rate constants, carbon use efficiencies) useful for calibrating fungal agents in ABMs.

### Network and graph-theoretic approaches

**Bebber et al. (2007).** Characterised the network architecture of fungal mycelia, showing that they form efficient transport networks with properties similar to engineered distribution systems. This provides a quantitative basis for modeling nutrient transport through fungal networks in ABMs.

**Heaton et al. (2012).** Modeled fungal networks as adaptive transport systems, showing that mycelia can solve optimisation problems (connecting food sources efficiently) through local growth rules. Relevant for ABM implementations where fungal agents must allocate resources across a spatial network.

### Gaps in the ABM literature

The most notable gap is the lack of integrated models that combine all three fungal strategies (saprotrophic, mycorrhizal, parasitic) within a single ecosystem ABM. Most models focus on one strategy in isolation. The key unresolved modeling challenge is representing:
1. The decomposition loop (dead matter --> bioavailable nutrients) with realistic rate-limiting by substrate quality.
2. The mycorrhizal network as a transport/redistribution layer connecting producer agents.
3. Parasitic fungi as density-dependent population regulators.

## System without fungi

The thought experiment of removing fungi reveals their systemic importance through the failures that would cascade:

**Immediate: Decomposition bottleneck.** Without saprotrophic fungi, lignin-rich organic matter (wood, tough leaf litter) stops decomposing efficiently. Bacteria can handle labile compounds (sugars, simple proteins) but cannot break down lignin. Dead organic matter accumulates. The locked nutrient stock grows while the bioavailable nutrient flow to soil collapses. This is not hypothetical — the Carboniferous period provides a geological-scale natural experiment: before fungi evolved efficient lignin decomposition, dead plant material accumulated as peat at rates sufficient to form the world's major coal deposits (Floudas et al. 2012, though see Nelsen et al. 2016 for caveats).

**Short-term: Producer nutrient starvation.** As nutrient cycling slows, soil nutrient stocks deplete. Producers can no longer access sufficient nitrogen and phosphorus for growth. Net primary productivity declines. This is compounded by the loss of mycorrhizal networks, which in many ecosystems are responsible for 80-90% of plant phosphorus uptake (van der Heijden et al. 1998). Plants that depend on mycorrhizal phosphorus acquisition — which is most of them — would face severe nutrient limitation even if soil stocks were adequate, because they lack the root architecture to access those nutrients independently.

**Medium-term: Trophic cascade.** Declining producer biomass reduces energy input to the entire grazer pathway. Consumer populations decline. The system's total energy throughput drops because the tap (solar flux captured by producers) has been throttled by nutrient limitation, while the drain (metabolic costs) continues.

**Long-term: System state shift.** The ecosystem shifts from a nutrient-cycling steady state to a nutrient-accumulating state. Carbon and nutrients pile up in dead organic matter rather than flowing through living systems. Soil becomes increasingly oligotrophic (nutrient-poor in available forms, even as total nutrient content in locked organic matter may be high). The system converges toward a low-productivity, low-diversity state dominated by whatever producers can survive without mycorrhizal support and with minimal nutrient cycling.

**Loss of diversity maintenance.** Without parasitic fungi enforcing density-dependent mortality, dominant species face less biotic resistance to competitive exclusion. Species diversity declines as competitive dominants monopolise resources without pathogen-mediated setbacks.

The overall pattern: removing fungi doesn't eliminate energy from the system — solar flux still arrives. It breaks the nutrient loops that allow that energy to be captured and recirculated. The system doesn't run out of energy; it runs out of the molecular building blocks needed to capture energy.
