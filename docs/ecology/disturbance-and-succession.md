# Disturbance and Succession

Ecosystems are not static endpoints but dynamic systems that are periodically reset by disturbance and directionally reassembled through succession. Disturbance destroys standing stocks, releasing resources and creating space; succession is the process by which biological communities reclaim that space. Together, disturbance and succession generate the temporal heterogeneity that maintains diversity, drives adaptive radiation, and prevents competitive exclusion from collapsing complex communities into monocultures.

## Disturbance as a system concept

Disturbance is not damage in an evaluative sense — it is a discrete event that resets stocks and restructures flows. White & Pickett (1985) defined disturbance as "any relatively discrete event in time that disrupts ecosystem, community, or population structure and changes resources, substrate availability, or the physical environment." The key word is *discrete*: disturbance is punctuated against a background of continuous processes. A tree falling is disturbance; the slow decline in soil nitrogen that preceded the fall is not.

From a stocks-and-flows perspective, disturbance operates on stocks directly:

- **Biomass destruction.** Standing biomass (a stock of energy and nutrients) is partially or wholly destroyed. The stored energy is either released rapidly (fire) or transferred to the detrital pool (windthrow, disease mortality).
- **Resource release.** Nutrients locked in biomass become available. Light reaches the ground. Space opens. The flows that were suppressed by the prior occupants are unblocked.
- **Physical restructuring.** Substrate may be rearranged (flood, landslide), creating new spatial heterogeneity in resource availability.

The critical concept is the **disturbance regime** — not the individual event, but the statistical characterization of disturbance as a recurring system parameter. A disturbance regime is defined by:

- **Frequency**: how often disturbance recurs (fire return interval, flood recurrence).
- **Intensity**: the energy or force of the disturbance event per unit area per unit time.
- **Severity**: the proportion of stocks destroyed (distinct from intensity — a low-intensity fire in dry grassland can have high severity).
- **Extent**: the spatial area affected.
- **Type**: the mechanism (fire, wind, flood, biotic outbreak).

The disturbance regime is a system parameter in the same way that mean temperature or soil fertility is — it selects for certain life history strategies and against others. Organisms evolve not in response to individual disturbance events but to the statistical properties of the regime (Pickett & White 1985).

**The intermediate disturbance hypothesis** (Connell 1978) proposes that species diversity is maximized at intermediate levels of disturbance. At low disturbance frequency, competitive exclusion proceeds to completion and a few dominant species monopolize resources. At high disturbance frequency, only fast-colonizing ruderals persist. At intermediate frequency, both competitive dominants and colonizers coexist because neither process runs to completion. The hypothesis has been debated extensively (Fox 2013, Sheil & Burslem 2013), and its generality depends on the relationship between competitive ability and colonization ability, but the core insight — that both too much and too little disturbance reduce diversity — remains foundational.

## Types of disturbance

Different disturbance types have different effects on ecosystem stocks and flows. What matters from a systems perspective is not the proximate mechanism but which stocks are affected, how fast, and what resource pulses result.

### Fire

Fire is the rapid oxidation of standing biomass — a conversion of the energy stock from reduced carbon to heat and CO2. From a flows perspective, fire:

- **Releases energy catastrophically.** Decades of accumulated NPP are converted to heat in minutes to hours.
- **Creates a nutrient pulse.** Ash contains mineralized nutrients (especially P, K, Ca) that were locked in biomass and litter. Nitrogen is largely volatilized and lost, creating a post-fire nutrient imbalance that favors nitrogen-fixers in early succession (Raison 1979).
- **Resets the light environment.** Canopy removal floods the ground layer with light, triggering germination of fire-dependent seed banks and rapid growth of surviving rootstocks.
- **Is spatially heterogeneous.** Fire intensity varies with fuel load, moisture, and topography, creating a mosaic of burn severities. This spatial heterogeneity in disturbance severity translates directly to heterogeneity in successional stage.

Fire is distinctive because it is a self-propagating disturbance — it generates the conditions for its own spread. Fire behavior is therefore fundamentally a spatial-contagion process, amenable to cellular automata and percolation models (Drossel & Schwabl 1992).

### Predation and herbivory outbreaks

Biotic disturbances — insect outbreaks, disease epidemics, irruptions of herbivores — represent spikes in trophic flow that draw down a particular biomass stock. Spruce budworm outbreaks kill canopy trees over millions of hectares (Ludwig, Jones & Holling 1978). Locust swarms strip vegetation to bare ground. These events transfer biomass from the living stock to the detrital stock, creating a resource pulse for decomposers and opening canopy gaps.

Biotic disturbances differ from abiotic ones in a critical way: they are density-dependent. Outbreaks require a threshold host density, and they self-terminate when the host stock is depleted. This creates an endogenous disturbance cycle — the host population grows, crosses the outbreak threshold, is reduced by the disturbance, and begins growing again. The spruce budworm system is a classic example of relaxation oscillations driven by this coupling (Ludwig, Jones & Holling 1978).

### Disease

Disease acts as a selective stock reduction — it removes individuals from the population based on susceptibility, which may correlate with genotype, condition, or density. Unlike fire, disease typically leaves physical structure intact while removing the organisms that created it. Standing dead trees from disease outbreaks provide habitat (snags for cavity nesters) and slowly transfer nutrients through decomposition rather than rapid combustion.

Pathogen-mediated disturbance can be frequency-dependent (Janzen-Connell effects), where the probability of mortality increases with conspecific density, creating negative feedback on any single species' dominance and thereby promoting diversity (Janzen 1970, Connell 1971).

### Physical disturbance

Wind, flood, and mass movement operate on physical structure directly:

- **Wind** topples trees, creating pit-and-mound microtopography that generates spatial heterogeneity in soil moisture, light, and nutrients. Windthrow transfers the entire biomass stock to the detrital pool without combustion.
- **Flood** redistributes substrate, scours organisms from surfaces, deposits sediment, and creates new bare surfaces. Flood disturbance is distinctive in its directional transport — material moves downstream — and its gradient of severity from channel to floodplain.
- **Landslides and volcanic events** represent primary disturbance — removal of the entire biotic substrate, resetting the system to bare rock or mineral soil.

Each physical disturbance type has a characteristic spatial signature. Wind produces linear or patch disturbance oriented to prevailing storm tracks. Floods produce linear disturbance along drainage networks. Landslides produce slope-oriented patches. This spatial structure determines the geometry of the successional mosaic.

## Succession

Succession is the directional, broadly predictable change in community composition following disturbance. It is the process by which the space and resources released by disturbance are reclaimed by organisms.

### Primary vs secondary succession

**Primary succession** begins on substrates that have never supported a biological community or from which all biological legacy has been removed — new volcanic lava, glacial till, bare rock. There is no soil, no seed bank, no surviving root network. Primary succession is slow (centuries to millennia) because organisms must build the substrate they need: lichens weather rock, pioneer plants create organic soil, each stage generating the conditions for the next.

**Secondary succession** begins on substrates where disturbance has destroyed the community but left biological legacy intact — seed banks in soil, surviving rootstocks, organic soil, microbial communities. Secondary succession is fast (years to decades) because the substrate and propagule supply already exist. Most disturbance in established ecosystems triggers secondary succession.

### Models of succession

Connell & Slatyer (1977) proposed three mechanistic models:

**Facilitation.** Early-arriving species modify the environment in ways that benefit later-arriving species and harm themselves. Pioneer plants fix nitrogen, build soil, create shade — conditions that favor shade-tolerant competitors who eventually replace them. This is Clements' (1916) relay floristics model formalized: each seral stage facilitates the next in a deterministic sequence toward climax.

**Tolerance.** Early and late species are equally capable of establishing, but later species tolerate lower resource levels. Succession proceeds as resources are drawn down by initial colonizers, and species that can persist at lower resource levels gradually replace those that cannot. This model predicts a sequence determined by competitive ability along a resource depletion gradient (Tilman 1988).

**Inhibition.** Early-arriving species resist invasion by later species through priority effects — they hold space, monopolize resources, and prevent establishment until they are themselves removed by disturbance or senescence. Under inhibition, succession is slow and driven not by deterministic replacement but by the lifespan and mortality of the initial colonizers. The sequence is contingent on who arrives first.

In practice, all three mechanisms operate simultaneously in most systems, and their relative importance varies with the resource in question, the spatial scale, and the organisms involved.

### Clements vs Gleason

The debate between Clements (1916) and Gleason (1926) frames a fundamental question: is succession deterministic or stochastic?

**Clements** viewed the community as a superorganism developing toward a predetermined climax state determined by regional climate. Succession was ontogeny at the community level — predictable, directional, convergent.

**Gleason** viewed the community as an accidental assemblage of species with overlapping environmental tolerances. Succession was the individualistic response of species to changing conditions — contingent, variable, divergent.

Modern ecology sits closer to Gleason. Community assembly is stochastic, influenced by dispersal limitation, priority effects, and historical contingency. But directional change does occur because environmental modification by organisms is real (facilitation), and species do differ systematically in their competitive abilities and life history strategies. The resolution is that succession has both deterministic and stochastic components: the direction of change is broadly predictable, but the specific species composition at any point is contingent (Walker & del Moral 2003).

## Alternative stable states

A critical insight from disturbance ecology is that ecosystems can exist in multiple stable configurations — **alternative stable states** — and disturbance can flip systems between them.

Scheffer et al. (2001) synthesized evidence across ecosystem types:

- **Shallow lakes** can be clear (dominated by rooted macrophytes that stabilize sediments and suppress algae) or turbid (dominated by phytoplankton that shade out macrophytes and keep sediments resuspended). Both states are self-reinforcing.
- **Savannas and forests** on the same substrate under the same climate represent alternative states maintained by fire-vegetation feedback. Forest suppresses fire and maintains itself; savanna promotes fire and maintains itself (Staver, Archibald & Levin 2011).
- **Coral reefs and algal reefs** represent alternative states mediated by herbivory. Coral-dominated reefs support herbivorous fish that suppress algae. When herbivores are removed (overfishing), algae overgrow coral, coral dies, herbivore habitat is lost, and the system locks into an algal-dominated state (Hughes 1994).

The key concept is **hysteresis**: the disturbance intensity needed to flip from state A to state B is different from the intensity needed to flip from B back to A. In the lake example, nutrient loading must be reduced far below the level that caused the switch to turbidity before clear-water conditions return. This asymmetry means that a disturbance that pushes a system past a tipping point may require a much larger intervention to reverse.

In stocks-and-flows terms, alternative stable states arise when reinforcing feedback loops create multiple basins of attraction in the system's state space. Each basin represents a self-maintaining configuration. Disturbance moves the system through state space; if the perturbation is large enough to push the system over the rim of its current basin, it rolls into an alternative basin and locks in.

## Resilience and resistance

Holling (1973) introduced the concept of **ecological resilience** — the magnitude of disturbance a system can absorb before shifting to an alternative state. This is distinct from **engineering resilience**, which is the speed of return to equilibrium after a small perturbation.

The distinction matters:

- **Engineering resilience** (Pimm 1984) measures return time. A system that bounces back quickly is resilient in this sense. This concept assumes a single equilibrium and measures behavior near it.
- **Ecological resilience** (Holling 1973) measures the size of the basin of attraction — how far the system can be displaced and still return to the same state. A system can have high ecological resilience (large basin of attraction, tolerant of big disturbances) but low engineering resilience (slow return time). Old-growth forests exemplify this: they recover slowly from disturbance but can absorb large perturbations without flipping to a fundamentally different state.

**Resistance** is a related but distinct concept: the degree to which a system resists being displaced by disturbance in the first place. A system with high resistance barely moves in response to perturbation; a system with low resistance is easily displaced. High resistance does not imply high resilience — a resistant system may have a small basin of attraction and, once displaced past its resistance threshold, flip irreversibly.

Walker et al. (2004) formalized these ideas in the **adaptive cycle** framework (following Holling 1986), which describes ecosystems as cycling through four phases: rapid growth (r), conservation (K), release (omega), and reorganization (alpha). Disturbance triggers the release phase, which liberates stored resources. Reorganization is the period of maximum uncertainty, when the system can either return to its previous state or reorganize into a novel configuration. The adaptive cycle framework captures the idea that resilience itself changes through time — systems in the conservation phase accumulate resources and connections that make them simultaneously more resistant and more vulnerable to catastrophic release.

## Gap dynamics

Not all disturbance is landscape-scale catastrophe. Within otherwise stable ecosystems, small-scale disturbances — individual tree falls, burrow collapses, animal trampling — create **gaps** that function as local resets embedded within a matrix of undisturbed habitat.

**Forest gap dynamics** are the best-studied example. When a canopy tree dies, it creates a gap in which light reaches the forest floor. This light pulse triggers a local successional sequence: pioneer species establish, grow rapidly, and are eventually replaced by shade-tolerant species that close the canopy again. The forest as a whole is a shifting mosaic of gaps at different stages of closure (Watt 1947, Whitmore 1989).

Gap size determines successional trajectory:

- **Small gaps** (single treefall) are filled by lateral crown expansion of neighboring trees or by advance regeneration — shade-tolerant seedlings already established in the understory. Small gaps favor competitive dominants.
- **Large gaps** (multiple treefalls, lightning strikes) receive enough light to support shade-intolerant pioneers. Large gaps favor colonizers and early-successional species.

The distribution of gap sizes in a forest therefore determines the relative abundance of shade-tolerant vs shade-intolerant species and, consequently, overall community diversity. Forests with a wide range of gap sizes maintain higher diversity than forests dominated by either very small or very large gaps (Denslow 1987).

Gap dynamics provide the spatial unit of succession. The forest is not successional as a whole — it is a mosaic of patches, each at its own successional stage, with diversity maintained by the asynchrony of local disturbance-recovery cycles across the landscape.

## The disturbance regime and diversity

Many ecosystems require disturbance to prevent competitive exclusion from collapsing diversity. Without periodic reset, succession proceeds to a stable state dominated by a few strong competitors. Disturbance interrupts this process, maintaining the system in a non-equilibrium state where multiple species coexist.

**Fire-dependent ecosystems.** Longleaf pine savannas, Mediterranean shrublands (chaparral, maquis, fynbos), and boreal forests depend on periodic fire. Many species in these systems have fire-adapted traits: serotinous cones that open only after fire (lodgepole pine), resprouting from underground lignotubers (chaparral shrubs), fire-stimulated germination (many fynbos species), thick insulating bark (ponderosa pine, longleaf pine). Fire suppression in these systems leads to woody encroachment, canopy closure, loss of fire-adapted species, and fuel accumulation that makes eventual fire catastrophically severe (Pausas & Keeley 2009).

**Grazing-dependent grasslands.** Many grassland ecosystems are maintained by large herbivore grazing, which prevents competitive exclusion by dominant grasses and creates bare ground for forb establishment. African savannas, North American tallgrass prairies, and European meadows all depend on grazing disturbance. The removal of large herbivores leads to grass dominance, litter accumulation, and loss of forb diversity (Collins et al. 1998, Knapp et al. 1999).

**Flood-dependent riparian systems.** Riparian forests and wetlands depend on periodic flooding to redistribute sediment, scour vegetation, deposit propagules, and maintain the mosaic of bare ground, pioneer stands, and mature forest that characterizes healthy floodplains. River regulation (dams) eliminates flood disturbance and causes riparian communities to senesce toward a simplified, late-successional state (Tockner & Stanford 2002).

The common pattern: disturbance-dependent ecosystems have evolved under a specific disturbance regime, and their species are adapted to — indeed, require — that regime. Removing the disturbance does not "protect" the ecosystem; it destroys it by allowing competitive exclusion to proceed unchecked.

## Recovery trajectories

How a system reassembles after disturbance depends on what arrives, in what order, and what conditions it encounters. Recovery is not simply succession in reverse — it is a process of community assembly in a post-disturbance environment.

### Priority effects

The order of species arrival matters. Early-arriving species modify the environment (resources, physical structure, soil chemistry) in ways that affect which species can establish subsequently. **Priority effects** can be:

- **Facilitative**: early arrivals improve conditions for later species (nitrogen-fixers improving soil for later colonizers).
- **Inhibitory**: early arrivals preempt resources and resist displacement (dense stands of bracken fern preventing tree establishment).

When inhibitory priority effects are strong, recovery becomes path-dependent — the same site with the same species pool can assemble into different communities depending on which species arrived first (Chase 2003, Fukami 2015). This means that recovery from the same disturbance is not deterministic but contingent on stochastic events during the colonization window.

### Assembly rules

Community ecology has long sought "assembly rules" — constraints on which species combinations can coexist. Diamond (1975) proposed competitive exclusion-based assembly rules for bird communities. Keddy (1992) formalized the concept as environmental and biotic filters that progressively reduce the regional species pool to the local community.

In post-disturbance assembly:

1. **Dispersal filter**: only species whose propagules reach the site can establish. This depends on proximity to source populations, dispersal mechanisms, and landscape connectivity.
2. **Abiotic filter**: the post-disturbance environment (soil, light, moisture) excludes species that cannot tolerate those conditions.
3. **Biotic filter**: interactions among establishing species (competition, facilitation, predation) further constrain community composition.

The order of these filters and the stochasticity of dispersal mean that recovery trajectories are partially predictable (by abiotic conditions and functional trait requirements) and partially contingent (on dispersal history and priority effects).

### Time scales

Different ecosystem components recover on different time scales:

- **Microbial communities**: weeks to months (rapid growth, high dispersal).
- **Herbaceous vegetation**: months to years.
- **Shrub layer**: years to decades.
- **Canopy trees**: decades to centuries.
- **Soil organic matter**: decades to centuries.
- **Full species complement**: centuries (for primary succession on new substrate, millennia).

This temporal layering means that a system can appear recovered (canopy closure, biomass restoration) while still lacking the full structural and compositional complexity of the pre-disturbance state. Old-growth attributes — large dead wood, canopy epiphytes, specialized soil communities — accumulate on the slowest time scales.

## Feedback loops

Disturbance and succession are not one-directional processes but are embedded in feedback loops that can amplify, dampen, or redirect successional trajectories.

### Disturbance-diversity feedback

Disturbance creates heterogeneity. Heterogeneity supports diversity. Diverse communities may modify disturbance regimes (e.g., diverse forests may be less flammable than monocultures due to variation in fuel properties). This creates a potential reinforcing loop: disturbance promotes diversity, which modifies disturbance in ways that maintain diversity.

Conversely, competitive exclusion reduces diversity, which can homogenize fuel structure and increase the severity (though not necessarily frequency) of disturbance — a different kind of reinforcing loop that can lock systems into boom-bust cycles.

### Fire-vegetation feedback

The fire-vegetation feedback is the most studied disturbance feedback:

- **Grass-fire cycle.** Grasses produce fine fuel that cures rapidly and burns readily. Fire kills woody seedlings but not grass roots. The result is a reinforcing loop: grass promotes fire promotes grass (D'Antonio & Vitousek 1992). Invasion by flammable grasses into previously non-flammable systems (e.g., cheatgrass in North American sagebrush steppe) can trigger a state shift via this feedback.
- **Forest fire suppression.** Closed-canopy forest shades out grasses, maintains high humidity at ground level, and produces coarse woody fuel that is less flammable. The result is a reinforcing loop that suppresses fire and maintains forest. But this loop can be broken by severe drought or logging that opens the canopy, at which point grass invades and the grass-fire loop takes over.

### Recovery-facilitation feedback

During succession, early-successional species that facilitate later species create a reinforcing loop: facilitation accelerates succession, changing conditions in ways that accelerate further facilitation. Nitrogen-fixing pioneers improve soil, enabling faster growth of non-fixers, whose litter further improves soil. This loop drives the rapid phase of secondary succession. It is self-limiting — facilitation ceases to accelerate succession once resource levels reach the point where competitive interactions dominate.

### Ecosystem engineering feedback

Organisms can modify their own disturbance regime — ecosystem engineering that feeds back to disturbance frequency and intensity:

- **Beavers** create dams that reduce flood disturbance downstream while creating wetland habitat upstream — engineering a disturbance regime that benefits the engineer (Jones, Lawton & Shachak 1994).
- **Fire-promoting plants** (many Australian eucalypts, African grasses) produce biomass that is disproportionately flammable, increasing fire frequency and intensity in ways that favor fire-adapted species over competitors (Bond & Midgley 1995, Mutch 1970).
- **Burrowing animals** create physical disturbance that turns over soil, buries litter, and creates microsites for seedling establishment — small-scale ecosystem engineers that maintain spatial heterogeneity.

These engineering feedbacks mean that the disturbance regime is not purely exogenous — it is co-determined by the organisms living under it. The disturbance regime shapes the community, and the community shapes the disturbance regime.

## ABM and computational literature

Agent-based models are natural tools for disturbance-succession dynamics because they capture the individual-level stochasticity, spatial heterogeneity, and local interactions that drive these processes.

### Forest gap models

The forest gap model tradition — beginning with JABOWA (Botkin, Janak & Wallis 1972) and branching into FORET (Shugart & West 1977), SORTIE (Pacala et al. 1996), FORMIND (Kohler & Huth 1998, Fischer et al. 2016), and iLand (Seidl et al. 2012) — is fundamentally a disturbance-succession modeling framework. These models simulate individual trees competing for light and nutrients on small plots. Disturbance (tree mortality from senescence, wind, fire, insects) creates gaps. Succession is an emergent property of individual growth, death, and recruitment dynamics.

Key features of gap models relevant to disturbance-succession:

- **Individual-based mortality** creates realistic gap-size distributions.
- **Species-specific shade tolerance** drives successional replacement without explicit successional rules.
- **Stochastic recruitment** introduces contingency — the same gap colonized by different species produces different trajectories.
- **Scaling from plot to landscape** by simulating many independent gaps produces landscape-level diversity patterns.

Shugart (1984) demonstrated that forest gap models reproduce observed successional sequences, species composition, and biomass dynamics from first principles — no succession rules are encoded; succession emerges from individual trees living and dying.

### Fire spread models

Computational fire models range from simple cellular automata (Drossel & Schwabl 1992) to detailed physics-based models. For ecological purposes, the key models are:

- **Cellular automata fire models** treat each cell as vegetated or empty, with fire spreading stochastically to neighboring vegetated cells. Despite their simplicity, these models reproduce observed fire-size distributions (power-law scaling) and the self-organized criticality of fire regimes (Malamud, Morein & Turcotte 1998).
- **LANDIS** and related landscape disturbance models (Mladenoff 2004) couple fire spread with forest succession, allowing fire regime and vegetation to co-evolve. These models demonstrate that fire-vegetation feedbacks can produce alternative stable states at landscape scales.

### Disturbance-recovery in spatial ABMs

More generally, spatial agent-based models capture disturbance-recovery dynamics whenever agents die and are replaced. The critical model features are:

- **Spatial structure**: disturbance is local, and recovery depends on the neighborhood. Dispersal limitation and local interactions produce spatial patterns (clusters, gradients, mosaics) that purely mean-field models miss.
- **Heterogeneity**: agents differ in traits (growth rate, disturbance tolerance, dispersal ability), and this variation is the raw material for successional differentiation.
- **Stochasticity**: random mortality, random dispersal, random disturbance timing and location. Stochastic events during the recovery window (priority effects) produce path-dependent trajectories.
- **Emergence**: successional sequences, alternative stable states, and disturbance-diversity relationships are not encoded but emerge from local rules operating on heterogeneous, stochastic agents.

The ABM literature on disturbance-succession aligns with the general ABM principle that complex system-level patterns (succession, resilience, alternative states) arise from simple individual-level rules (grow, compete, die, reproduce, disperse) operating in a spatial, stochastic context (Grimm & Railsback 2005, DeAngelis & Mooij 2005).

### Disturbance as agent action

In models where agents can modify their environment, disturbance can be an agent-level action rather than an exogenous event. An agent that consumes resources, destroys habitat structure, or alters disturbance probability (e.g., by producing flammable biomass) is a disturbance agent — an ecosystem engineer whose actions ripple through the community via disturbance-succession dynamics. Any mobile organism that resets local stocks and triggers local succession functions as a disturbance source. Whether such an organism becomes integrated into the ecosystem depends on whether its disturbance regime (frequency, intensity, spatial pattern) falls within the parameter space that maintains diversity — or pushes the system past a tipping point into an impoverished alternative state.
